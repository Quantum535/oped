# One Shot, One Swab, One Visit: The Cancer We Refuse to End

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Cervical cancer is the closest thing oncology has to a solved problem. We know exactly what causes it. We have a vaccine that prevents it. We have a test that catches it years before it turns dangerous, and a treatment a nurse can deliver in a single visit. No other major cancer offers that complete a toolkit.

And yet the [comparative modelling analysis in The Lancet](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(20)30068-4) that underpins the World Health Organization's elimination strategy projects that girls-only vaccination, on its own, reaches elimination in only about [60 percent of 78 low-income and lower-middle-income countries](https://cdn.who.int/media/docs/default-source/cervical-cancer/brisson-et-al-2020-uploaded.pdf), and that in the regions where it does work, elimination arrives somewhere between 2074 and 2102. In the highest-burden countries, mostly in sub-Saharan Africa, the models are blunt: vaccination alone never gets there at all. Screening is not optional in those places. It is the difference between elimination and no elimination.

Read those dates again. On the current trajectory, a girl born today in the wrong country could plausibly die of cervical cancer as an old woman, of a disease whose cause was identified before her mother was born.

For years, the response to those timelines has been a dismissal offered as realism: vaccines are expensive, screening requires clinics and speculum exams and laboratory infrastructure, health budgets are finite. Every one of those excuses was priced on numbers that have since collapsed.

**The tools got cheaper. The excuses did not.**

In April 2022 the WHO's expert advisory group reviewed the trial evidence and concluded that a single dose provides protection comparable to two; the recommendation was formalized in the organization's [December 2022 position paper](https://www.who.int/news/item/20-12-2022-WHO-updates-recommendations-on-HPV-vaccination-schedule). At a stroke, the same vials reach twice as many girls, and the hardest logistical problem in adolescent immunization, getting each child back for a second visit, disappears.

Countries did not wait. Thirty-seven were using a one-dose schedule in 2023; 57 by September 2024; and [89 countries as of January 2026](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/ivac/2026/celebrating-recent-advances-in-cervical-cancer-prevention), a majority of every country that has introduced the vaccine at all. The effect is already measurable rather than theoretical: WHO credits the switch with reaching [at least 6 million additional girls in 2023 alone](https://www.who.int/news/item/04-10-2024-who-adds-an-hpv-vaccine-for-single-dose-use), as first-dose coverage among girls aged 9 to 14 rose from 20 to 27 percent in a single year.

Screening changed just as fundamentally. [WHO's 2021 guidelines](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK572321/) make an HPV test the primary screening tool and permit self-collection, so the sample no longer requires a speculum, a pelvic exam, or a clinician. That converts screening from a scarce clinical service into something closer to a distribution problem, and it matters most for the roughly nine in ten women who test negative and now need no examination at all.

For the women who do test positive, the referral chain that used to lose them has been compressed into one visit. Under WHO's screen-and-treat approach, a trained primary health worker examines the cervix, confirms the lesion is eligible for ablation, and treats it on the spot with a [portable thermal ablation device, typically without anesthesia](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK549176/). One visit, one provider, no colposcopy suite, no specialist referral, no second appointment at which most programs historically lost their patients.

Every major input has fallen: one dose instead of two, a self-collected swab instead of a clinic visit, one treatment encounter instead of a referral chain. And the same Lancet models show what the full toolkit buys. Adding twice-lifetime screening to vaccination raises elimination from 60 percent of those 78 countries to all of them, and pulls the date forward by 11 to 31 years. The science was never the obstacle. Now the economics is not either.

**The honest caveats, answered**

Three objections deserve real answers rather than slogans.

*Does one dose last?* This is the genuine uncertainty, and it is load-bearing: the Lancet models state plainly that long-term vaccine protection is required for elimination. The evidence is reassuring but not yet lifetime-long. In the Costa Rica vaccine trial, a single dose produced [HPV-16/18 antibodies still present 16 years later](https://academic.oup.com/jncimono/article/2024/67/329/7821493) with only small declines, and at 11 years a single dose was about 82 percent effective against the high-risk types, statistically indistinguishable from the 84 percent and 80 percent seen with two and three doses. That is strong enough to act on and thin enough to keep measuring, which is precisely why one-dose programs should be paired with serological follow-up rather than treated as settled.

*Does self-sampling break the link to treatment?* It can, which is why screen-and-treat protocols exist. The design point is that a positive result triggers a single treatment visit rather than a referral, and programs should be graded on the interval between result and ablation, not on swabs distributed.

*Is national rollout too complex?* Gavi and the Global Fund financed harder logistical revolutions in routine immunization and HIV treatment across two decades. The obstacle is not complexity. It is that cervical cancer elimination has been funded as a series of demonstration projects rather than as infrastructure.

**Australia is instructive, not magic**

Australia is the country furthest along, and it is worth being precise about what that does and does not prove. It has not eliminated cervical cancer yet: national incidence was [6.3 per 100,000 women in 2021](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-rebecca-white-mp/media/australia-on-track-to-eliminate-cervical-cancer-by-2035), against an elimination threshold of fewer than 4, and the government projects reaching that threshold [by 2035](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-rebecca-white-mp/media/australia-on-track-to-eliminate-cervical-cancer-by-2035).

Some of Australia's advantages are not transferable. It is a high-income country with universal health coverage, and pretending otherwise insults the ministries being lectured. But the decisions that produced the trajectory are transferable, and they are unglamorous: school-based delivery that reaches girls where they already are, a national register that knows who is due, an early switch from cytology to primary HPV testing, and self-collection offered as a standard option rather than a concession.

The most useful thing about Australia is its failure. Cervical cancer incidence among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women runs at [roughly twice the national rate](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/article/PIIS2468-2667(26)00005-8/fulltext), driven by long-standing inequity in screening access. A national program built for the median woman does not automatically reach the women furthest from care; it has to be designed to. That is the lesson high-burden countries should import, and it is the one most likely to be skipped.

The global picture confirms that the gap is organizational, not technological. A [Lancet Global Health review of 202 countries](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2214-109X(22)00241-8) found that among women aged 30 to 49, 84 percent in high-income countries had ever been screened, against 9 percent in lower-middle-income countries and 11 percent in low-income ones. And the trajectory is widening: a [global modelling study published in The Lancet in May 2026](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(26)00410-1/abstract) projects that incidence in lower-income countries, already about three times that of wealthier ones, could reach a twelvefold difference by the end of the century without stronger vaccination and screening.

**The budget line is shrinking, not waiting**

Here is what makes this urgent rather than merely regrettable. The argument that elimination is affordable is now colliding with a financing system moving the other way.

At its [June 2025 pledging summit](https://www.gavi.org/news/media-room/world-leaders-recommit-immunisation-amid-global-funding-shortfall), Gavi raised more than $9 billion against an $11.9 billion target for 2026 through 2030, leaving a gap of roughly $2.9 billion against a plan that includes reaching 120 million girls with HPV vaccine by 2030. The United States, historically Gavi's third-largest government donor and the source of about 13 percent of its funding since inception, [has not honored the roughly $1.6 billion pledged for that period](https://www.kff.org/global-health-policy/the-u-s-government-gavi-the-vaccine-alliance/); Congress appropriated $300 million in each of fiscal 2025 and 2026, and the administration has not disbursed it.

So the sentence "this is affordable" is true and insufficient. The cheapest moment in the history of cervical cancer prevention is arriving at the same time as a contraction in the money that buys it. A disease that could be finished within a generation is instead being handed a slower timeline by budget decisions made this year.

**Stop piloting. Start finishing.**

Countries still on two-dose schedules should switch now; a majority already have. Screening programs should make self-collection plus same-visit treatment the default model rather than the demonstration project. Gavi should carry cervical cancer elimination as a protected multi-year line rather than a renewable experiment, and donors, the United States included, should fund the pledges already made.

And every program should be graded on the three numbers WHO's 90-70-90 targets already name: girls fully vaccinated by age 15, women screened with a high-performance test by 35 and again by 45, and women found to have cervical disease who are actually treated, counting both precancer treated and invasive cancer managed.

A disease preventable by one shot, detectable by one swab, and treatable in one visit is not waiting on science. It is waiting on a budget line that is currently being cut. The next century of cervical cancer cases will not be evidence that the disease was hard to stop. They will be evidence that we declined to.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **National health ministries still using two-dose schedules:** Adopt the WHO one-dose schedule and publish a switch date. Eighty-nine countries have already moved; the evidence base, the cost case, and the supply agreements are all in place. Report first-dose coverage for girls aged 9 to 14 annually, and pair adoption with serological follow-up cohorts so durability continues to be measured rather than assumed.

2. **Gavi:** Convert cervical cancer elimination from campaign-cycle funding into a protected ten-year budget line covering single-dose vaccine, self-collection kits, and portable thermal ablation devices. Publish, against the 120-million-girl target for 2030, what the $2.9 billion replenishment shortfall removes in girls not vaccinated and in years added to elimination timelines.

3. **The United States Congress and administration:** Disburse the $300 million appropriated for Gavi in each of fiscal 2025 and 2026 and restore the 2026–2030 pledge. This is the rare global health commitment with a published, modelled return: elimination decades earlier in countries where vaccination alone will otherwise never achieve it.

4. **National screening programs in high-burden countries:** Make HPV self-collection plus same-visit thermal ablation the default protocol. Set and publish one metric that matters most, the share of screen-positive women treated within 30 days of result, with no referral step, and design outreach explicitly for the women furthest from care rather than the easiest to reach.

5. **WHO member states:** Replace pilot-site reporting with the three 90-70-90 numbers, disaggregated by geography, income, and indigenous or minority status. Australia's remaining twofold gap for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women is the proof that national averages conceal exactly the populations elimination requires reaching.
