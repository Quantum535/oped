# 200,000 Women a Year Die of a Breast Cancer We Already Know How to Cure

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

A woman diagnosed with breast cancer in North America has roughly an 84 percent chance of being alive in five years. A woman diagnosed with the identical cancer in parts of the low-income world faces five-year survival as low as 40 percent. The tumor is the same. The biology is the same. The only variable that changed is the country printed on her address, because breast cancer survival today is less a medical statistic than a map of national income.

Roughly 200,000 women a year die of a disease the evidence already knows how to catch and cure. That is not a research problem. It is a financing problem, and it is one the world has solved before, against a harder disease.

**The evidence is no longer in dispute**

According to a modelling study in The Lancet Global Health, survival in low- and middle-income settings clusters between 40 and 60 percent, and the gains appear only when early detection and treatment are built together. CONCORD-3, the largest cancer survival study ever conducted, spanning 37.5 million patient records across 71 countries, confirmed the gap is not closing on its own. And a February 2026 Lancet Global Health analysis of avoidable cancer deaths delivered the starkest finding of all: breast cancer now accounts for more treatable deaths than any other cancer on earth.

The deaths concentrate where anyone would predict: in countries where a woman can receive a diagnosis she cannot complete, because the screening program that found her tumor was never attached to a treatment system that could remove it.

**We have run this play before**

Two decades ago, treating HIV in Africa was widely dismissed as impossible. The drugs cost more than $10,000 a year. The health systems were too weak. The logistics were too complex. Then PEPFAR and the Global Fund did four unglamorous things at once: they financed on a horizon of decades rather than grant cycles, pooled procurement until generic drug prices collapsed below $100 a year, built a delivery workforce instead of waiting for one, and measured relentlessly against outcomes rather than activity. AIDS deaths have since fallen by more than half from their peak, with over 20 million people on treatment through PEPFAR alone.

Breast cancer is, in important ways, an easier target than AIDS ever was. There is no drug-pricing war to fight: tamoxifen and most first-line chemotherapy agents went off-patent decades ago and cost pennies to manufacture. The clinical protocols are among the most validated in all of oncology. What is missing is precisely what the AIDS playbook was built to supply: sustained financing, procurement scale, and workforce.

**Pay for outcomes, not equipment**

The World Health Organization's Global Breast Cancer Initiative has already defined the scoreboard: 60 percent of cancers diagnosed at an early stage, diagnostic workup completed within 60 days, and 80 percent of patients completing treatment. Funders should pay against those numbers, not against mammography units delivered. A screening van is an input. A woman who finishes treatment is an outcome. Global health learned that distinction fighting AIDS and has somehow declined to apply it to the cancer killing more treatable patients than any other.

The standard objections have answers. Can low-income countries support oncology infrastructure? Yes, if it is financed like infrastructure: a pathology workforce and a functioning referral network are ten-to-fifteen-year builds, and no infrastructure in history has been built on a three-year grant. Should detection come first and treatment follow? No. Screening without a working chemotherapy supply chain does not save a life; it converts a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one. Is pooled procurement too hard? Gavi pooled vaccines and the Global Fund pooled antiretrovirals. Off-patent cancer medicines are an easier list.

**One rule, applied everywhere**

Funders should anchor every breast cancer dollar to outcomes, pool procurement of the off-patent drug list, and enforce a single bright line: no detection rollout without a funded treatment endpoint already standing at the end of the referral pathway.

The AIDS response proved the world can build cancer-scale treatment systems in poor countries when it decides the deaths are unacceptable. Breast cancer's 200,000 avoidable deaths a year are waiting on that decision. The playbook exists. It has already beaten a harder disease. Open it.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **PEPFAR, the Global Fund, and USAID:** Shift breast cancer financing from three-year pilots to minimum ten-year commitments. Tie every new grant to the World Health Organization Global Breast Cancer Initiative targets: 60 percent of cases diagnosed at stage I or II, diagnostic workup completed within 60 days, and 80 percent of patients completing treatment.

2. **Gavi and Unitaid:** Launch pooled procurement for off-patent breast cancer medicines (tamoxifen, doxorubicin, cyclophosphamide, and the WHO Essential Medicines List oncology core) by 2028, using the same mechanism that cut antiretroviral prices below $100 per year.

3. **World Bank and bilateral health agencies:** Freeze procurement of mammography units, ultrasound devices, or mobile screening vans in any country that cannot document, at grant signing, a functioning treatment pathway (pathology, surgery, and chemotherapy) with capacity to start treatment within 60 days of diagnosis.

4. **Every global health donor:** Stop reporting "screenings delivered" as a primary outcome. Report instead the five-year survival rate in funded districts and the percentage of patients who complete treatment after a positive screen.
