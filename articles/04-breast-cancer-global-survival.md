# 200,000 Women a Year Die of a Breast Cancer We Already Know How to Cure

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

A woman in North America with breast cancer has roughly an 84% chance of living five years after the initial diagnosis. In contrast, a woman in lower-income nations with the exact same type of breast cancer (the same tumor, the same biological characteristics) has a five-year survival rate of just 40%. The only difference here is the country listed on the back of her driver's license, or put another way, there are many different "cures" for breast cancer today. While we know how to detect and cure the vast majority of these cases, millions of women each year will not survive their diagnosis.

It's not a research issue. This is primarily a funding problem. We have seen this play out before in global public health, specifically in combating HIV/AIDS. The evidence shows us that we know exactly how to prevent this disease. So why do hundreds of thousands of women continue to die of a curable disease?

**There is no longer a debate about the evidence**

[A modeling study published in The Lancet Global Health](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2214-109X(18)30257-2) demonstrates that survival rates cluster around 40% to 60% in lower-middle-income settings and that improvement only occurs when both early detection and treatment are implemented. Similarly, [CONCORD-3](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)33326-3), the largest cancer survival study ever conducted, which includes data from 37.5 million patients across 71 countries, demonstrated that the gap in survival rates is not narrowing on its own. Additionally, [an article published in Lancet Global Health in February 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2214-109X(25)00494-2) analyzing avoidable deaths due to cancer revealed that breast cancer currently causes more treatable deaths than any other form of cancer.

These deaths occur in locations where one would expect them to occur: in places where a woman receives a diagnosis but has limited access to follow-up care due to either lack of available treatments or inability to afford them. These issues arise when screening programs are established without any plans for follow-up treatment.

**This isn't new.**

Over two decades ago, treating HIV/AIDS in Africa was deemed virtually impossible by experts worldwide. The drugs required to treat the disease were estimated to be approximately $10,000 per year. Furthermore, the health systems lacked sufficient resources and capacity to administer these medications. Logistics also presented a significant barrier to widespread administration. However, through the concerted efforts of PEPFAR and the Global Fund, four unremarkable strategies were simultaneously implemented: they provided financing to support long-term implementation versus short-term grants; they created economies of scale in procurement to bring generic drug costs down below $100 per year; instead of waiting for a pre-existing workforce to develop, they invested in developing their own delivery workforce; and lastly, they focused on measuring progress based on outcomes rather than simply reporting activity. As a result of this collaborative effort, [AIDS-related deaths have decreased by more than half since their peak](https://www.unaids.org/en/resources/fact-sheet), and [over 20 million individuals have received treatment via PEPFAR alone](https://www.hiv.gov/federal-response/pepfar-global-aids/pepfar).

While breast cancer may present some advantages over AIDS, such as reduced competition for expensive drugs (most first-line chemotherapy agents and tamoxifen went off patent decades ago and can be manufactured for pennies), there remain three key areas in which funding is lacking: sustained financing, procurement scale, and workforce development.

**Don't fund equipment. Fund results.**

[The World Health Organization's Global Breast Cancer Initiative](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240065987) has identified several benchmarks for success: 60% of cancers diagnosed at an early stage; diagnostic workups completed within sixty days; and treatment completion rates above 80%. Funding agencies should incentivize achievement of these outcomes rather than focusing solely on delivering mammography machines. A screening van is an input. A woman who completes treatment is an output. Global health learned this lesson while battling AIDS. Unfortunately, it appears to have forgotten this approach when applying similar principles to the most lethal form of treatable cancer.

**There are responses to the typical counterarguments.**

Can lower-income countries establish adequate oncology infrastructure? Yes. If it is financed as such, that is, as infrastructure, then establishing a pathology workforce and implementing a functional referral network are both decade-long investments. No infrastructure in history has been developed using a three-year grant. Should detection precede treatment? No. Screening without an operational chemotherapy supply chain will not save lives; it merely converts late-stage diagnoses into earlier, fatal ones. Is pooled procurement too difficult? GAVI pooled vaccines and the Global Fund pooled ARVs. Creating a list of off-patent cancer medicines presents significantly fewer logistical challenges.

**One Rule Applied Everywhere**

Funding agencies should link every dollar used for breast cancer directly to measurable outcomes; pool procurement for the list of off-patent drugs; and enforce one clear rule: do not roll out detection programs without first establishing a funded treatment endpoint at the end of every referral pathway.

As proven by the AIDS response, we can build treatment systems capable of addressing large numbers of cancer patients in resource-constrained countries when we decide that the deaths are unacceptable. The 200,000 annual avoidable deaths resulting from breast cancer wait for that decision.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **PEPFAR, the Global Fund, and USAID:** Move all grants to a minimum ten-year commitment to fund the care of breast cancer patients. Tie funding to the WHO's Global Breast Cancer Initiative: 60% of all new cases diagnosed as Stage I or II; a full diagnostic workup completed within sixty (60) days of referral; and 80% of patients completing their treatment.

2. **GAVI and UNITAID:** By 2028, launch pooled procurement for generic versions of off-patent breast cancer medications (Tamoxifen, Doxorubicin, Cyclophosphamide, and other medicines on the WHO's Essential Medicines List Oncology Core), through the same pooled procurement mechanism used to bring HIV/AIDS medications below $100 per year.

3. **The World Bank and bilateral agencies:** Freeze the purchase of mammography equipment, ultrasound machines, and mobile screening vehicles for any country that cannot demonstrate, at the time of contract signature, a functional treatment pathway (pathology, surgery, chemotherapy) that can begin treatment within sixty (60) days of diagnosis.

4. **Every global health donor:** Stop listing "screenings delivered" as your main measure of success. Instead, report the five-year survival rate in your funded districts and the percentage of patients who successfully complete treatment after a positive result.
