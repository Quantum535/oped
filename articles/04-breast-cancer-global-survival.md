# GLOBAL BREAST CANCER NEEDS THE PLAYBOOK THAT BEAT AIDS

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Here's a statistic that should be a global scandal and instead barely
registers: a woman diagnosed with breast cancer in North America has
roughly an 84% chance of being alive in five years. A woman diagnosed
with the identical cancer in parts of the low-income world faces five-
year survival as low as 40%. Breast cancer does not become more lethal
when it crosses the equator. According to a modelling study in The
Lancet Global Health, the difference is whether anyone found the tumor
while it was still curable — and whether treatment existed to finish the
job once they did.

The evidence base for that claim is now unusually deep. The Lancet
Global Health modelling found survival in low- and middle-income
settings clustering between 40% and 60%, and found the gains come only
when early detection and treatment are built together — screening
without a working chemotherapy supply chain converts a late diagnosis
into an earlier, still-fatal one. CONCORD-3, the largest cancer survival
study ever conducted — 37.5 million patient records across 71 countries
— confirmed the gap isn't closing. And a February 2026 Lancet Global
Health analysis of avoidable cancer deaths found breast cancer accounts
for more treatable deaths than any other cancer on earth: roughly
200,000 women a year who die of a disease the evidence already knows how
to catch and cure, concentrated overwhelmingly in countries with low and
medium Human Development Index scores.

The usual response is that this is what poverty does, and time and
donations will slowly fix it. But global health has heard "infeasible"
before, about a harder problem, and demolished it. Two decades ago,
treating HIV in Africa was widely dismissed as impossible — the drugs
cost more than $10,000 a year, the health systems were too weak, the
logistics too complex. Then PEPFAR and the Global Fund did four
unglamorous things at once: financed on a horizon of decades rather than
grant cycles, pooled procurement until generic drug prices collapsed
below $100 a year, built a delivery workforce instead of waiting for
one, and measured relentlessly against outcomes rather than activity.
AIDS deaths have since fallen by more than half from their peak, with
over 20 million people on treatment through PEPFAR alone.

Breast cancer is, in important ways, an easier target than AIDS was.
There is no drug-pricing war to fight: tamoxifen and most first-line
chemotherapy agents went off-patent decades ago and cost pennies to
manufacture. The clinical protocols are among the most validated in all
of oncology. What's missing is precisely what the AIDS playbook was
built to supply — sustained financing, procurement scale, and workforce
— and no one has seriously applied it.

---

## What to Do

Applied to breast cancer, the playbook writes itself. Anchor the money
to outcomes, not equipment: the World Health Organization's Global
Breast Cancer Initiative has already defined the three targets — 60% of
cancers diagnosed at an early stage, diagnostic workup completed within
60 days, 80% of patients completing treatment — and funders should pay
against those numbers, not against mammography units delivered. Pool
procurement of the off-patent drug list the way Gavi pooled vaccines, so
no health ministry pays boutique prices for generic medicine. Enforce
the rule the Lancet modelling data demands: no detection rollout without
a funded treatment endpoint already standing at the end of the referral
pathway. And finance it in ten-to-fifteen-year commitments, because a
pathology workforce and a functioning referral network are
infrastructure, and infrastructure has never once been built on a three-
year grant.

---

A five-year survival rate is supposed to measure how good our medicine
is. For breast cancer it still measures how rich a woman's country was
when she got sick. The AIDS response proved that particular map can be
redrawn — deliberately, at scale, within a generation. Two hundred
thousand women a year are the cost of not opening the same playbook
twice.
