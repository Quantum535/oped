# GLOBAL BREAST CANCER NEEDS THE PLAYBOOK THAT BEAT AIDS

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

You are a woman in a low-income country. You find a lump in your breast. The nearest hospital can confirm the diagnosis, but chemotherapy is unavailable, the referral pathway is broken, and your five-year survival odds are less than half what they would be if you had been born elsewhere.

The solution is straightforward. Breast cancer in low- and middle-income countries is not more lethal because the biology changes at the equator. It is more lethal because detection and treatment are not built together. Yet global funders still finance screening pilots without guaranteeing treatment at the end of the referral line. The result is predictable: Roughly 200,000 women a year die of a disease the evidence already knows how to catch and cure.

Problem magnitude

A woman diagnosed with breast cancer in North America has roughly an 84 percent chance of being alive in five years. A woman diagnosed with the identical cancer in parts of the low-income world faces five-year survival as low as 40 percent. According to a modelling study in The Lancet Global Health, the difference is whether anyone found the tumor while it was still curable and whether treatment existed to finish the job once they did.

The evidence base for that claim is now unusually deep. The Lancet Global Health modelling found survival in low- and middle-income settings clustering between 40 and 60 percent, and found the gains come only when early detection and treatment are built together. CONCORD-3, the largest cancer survival study ever conducted, with 37.5 million patient records across 71 countries, confirmed the gap is not closing. Most importantly, a February 2026 Lancet Global Health analysis of avoidable cancer deaths found breast cancer accounts for more treatable deaths than any other cancer on earth.

The precedent that already worked

Two decades ago, treating HIV in Africa was widely dismissed as impossible. The drugs cost more than $10,000 a year. The health systems were too weak. The logistics were too complex. Then PEPFAR and the Global Fund did four unglamorous things at once: financed on a horizon of decades rather than grant cycles, pooled procurement until generic drug prices collapsed below $100 a year, built a delivery workforce instead of waiting for one, and measured relentlessly against outcomes rather than activity. AIDS deaths have since fallen by more than half from their peak, with over 20 million people on treatment through PEPFAR alone.

Breast cancer is, in important ways, an easier target than AIDS was. There is no drug-pricing war to fight. Tamoxifen and most first-line chemotherapy agents went off-patent decades ago and cost pennies to manufacture. The clinical protocols are among the most validated in all of oncology. What is missing is precisely what the AIDS playbook was built to supply: sustained financing, procurement scale, and workforce.

The financial case is equally compelling

An outcomes-based financing model costs far less than the lives lost each year. In comparison, one woman who dies of treatable breast cancer represents a failure that screening-without-treatment programs repeat daily. The World Health Organization's Global Breast Cancer Initiative has already defined three targets: 60 percent of cancers diagnosed at an early stage, diagnostic workup completed within 60 days, and 80 percent of patients completing treatment. Funders should pay against those numbers, not against mammography units delivered.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether low-income countries can support oncology infrastructure. The solution is simple: Finance it in ten-to-fifteen-year commitments, because a pathology workforce and a functioning referral network are infrastructure, and infrastructure has never once been built on a three-year grant.

Another concern is that detection programs should come first. Research shows the opposite of that sequencing when treatment is absent. Screening without a working chemotherapy supply chain converts a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one.

A third counterargument points to the cost of pooled drug procurement. However, only boutique pricing persists when health ministries buy alone. Gavi pooled vaccines. The same model applies to off-patent cancer medicines.

The path forward

Funders should anchor every breast cancer dollar to outcomes, pool procurement of the off-patent drug list, and enforce one rule: no detection rollout without a funded treatment endpoint already standing at the end of the referral pathway. The PEPFAR playbook exists. Apply it.

The central question for policymakers is whether we can afford to keep treating breast cancer survival as a map of national income. The case is clear and compelling: The science, the economics, and the precedent from the AIDS response all support action. Every global health funder should recognize integrated breast cancer care as a critical investment in saving lives and closing the survival gap.

## Take Action

Funders and health ministers should tie every breast cancer dollar to outcomes: early-stage diagnosis, workup within 60 days, and treatment completion. No new screening rollout without a funded treatment path already in place. The PEPFAR playbook exists. Apply it.
