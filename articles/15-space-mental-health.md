# The Plan for a Psychiatric Emergency in Orbit Is Duct Tape and a Syringe. Mars Needs Actual Psychiatry.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Buried in the International Space Station's 1,051-page medical checklist, five pages tell a crew what to do if one of their own suffers a psychotic break in orbit. The instructions, [disclosed by the Associated Press in 2007](https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna17300028), are these: bind the wrists and ankles with duct tape, secure the patient with a bungee cord, offer oral Haldol and Valium, and inject the drugs by force if refused. "Talk with the patient while you are restraining him," the checklist advises. "Explain what you are doing." The document surfaced days after astronaut Lisa Nowak's arrest during a post-flight personal crisis, an episode that forced NASA to review [how it screens and monitors the psychological health of the people it flies](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/nasa-has-plan-if-astronauts-lose-it/). Nearly two decades later, duct tape and a syringe remain, as far as anyone outside the agency has seen, the terminal line of defense.

That plan assumes what every other piece of space medicine assumes: that Earth is close. A restrained patient in low Earth orbit can be home in a day. The whole trajectory of human spaceflight is toward places where that stops being true, and the psychiatric problem, unlike the surgical one, is not a tail risk. It is the medical event NASA's own numbers say is more likely than not to happen.

**The mind has already cut missions short**

Spaceflight's psychiatric history is thin on headlines because it was written in euphemism, but it is there. In 1976, the Soviet [Soyuz 21 mission to the Salyut 5 station came home early](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soyuz_21), its flight engineer in deteriorating psychological condition after weeks of a punishing schedule aboard a station the crew believed was leaking toxic fumes. NASA's current behavioral-health evidence report notes that psychological problems [contributed to ending the 1985 Soyuz T-14 mission to Salyut 7 a full 160 days early](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/evidence/reports/HRP-ER-BMED-HFBP-20250210.pdf). In 1973, the overscheduled crew of Skylab 4 fell into a standoff with mission control that entered folklore as the first "strike in space"; [NASA's historians dispute the mutiny label](https://www.nasa.gov/history/the-real-story-of-the-skylab-4-strike-in-space/), but the workload conflict was real, and the in-flight renegotiation that resolved it became a founding case study in why crews need autonomy. On Mir in the late 1990s, [two of the seven NASA astronauts who flew long-duration missions reported subclinical depressive symptoms](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/evidence/reports/HRP-ER-BMED-HFBP-20250210.pdf). Russian space medicine developed an entire diagnostic vocabulary, asthenia, for the fatigue-irritability syndrome it kept observing.

The quiet in the American record is partly real resilience and partly an artifact of incentives. An astronaut's flight status is their career, and self-reporting a mood problem is the fastest way to lose it. The data NASA publishes, [nine documented behavioral-health events across the first forty ISS expeditions](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/evidence/reports/HRP-ER-BMED-HFBP-20250210.pdf), comes from flight-surgeon records, and the agency's own evidence report concedes it is likely an undercount for exactly that reason.

**NASA's own arithmetic says this is coming**

Look instead at what crews do rather than what they report. The largest sleep study ever conducted in space, [published in The Lancet Neurology in 2014](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laneur/article/PIIS1474-4422(14)70122-X/abstract), found astronauts averaging barely six hours of sleep a night and reaching for pharmacology to get it: [three-quarters of ISS crewmembers used sleep medication](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2014/08/140807215803.htm) at some point in flight, and shuttle crews used it on more than half of all nights in space. The station's pharmacy quietly stocks the rest of the psychiatric toolkit: [sertraline and venlafaxine for depression, diazepam and lorazepam for anxiety, aripiprazole and ziprasidone for psychosis](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20230003796/downloads/NASA-TP-2023003796%20SSRI%20Manuscript.pdf). We do not talk about mental illness in space, but we pack for it.

When NASA models longer missions, the numbers stop being subtle. Extrapolating ordinary civilian incidence rates across the roughly 120 person-years astronauts had logged by 2016, the agency's integrated medical model estimated an [85 percent chance that a diagnosable anxiety disorder has already occurred in flight](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/evidence/reports/HRP-ER-BMED-HFBP-20250210.pdf), somewhere, in someone, most likely undetected. For Mars, the evidence report reaches for the closest Earth analog we have, Antarctic winter-over crews, and the result should be pinned to the wall of every mission-architecture meeting: under the assumptions NASA cites, the probability of a serious behavioral problem on a Mars mission runs [as high as 89 percent for the long-stay design](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/evidence/reports/HRP-ER-BMED-HFBP-20250210.pdf). The agency's [formal risk register](https://humanresearchroadmap.nasa.gov/risks/risk.aspx?i=99) says the quiet part in bureaucratic prose: feasible and acceptable countermeasures for exploration missions "have not yet been developed and validated."

The rehearsals agree with the math. When six volunteers spent 520 days locked in a Moscow warehouse simulating a Mars round trip, the [Mars-500 experiment](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1212646110), one crewmember reported depressive symptoms in 93 percent of mission weeks, another developed chronic sleep deprivation with measurable lapses in alertness, and the crew as a whole sank into lethargy, moving less and sleeping more as the months ground on. Only two of the six came through without behavioral disturbance. Antarctic research stations, our best standing analog, reliably produce the "[third-quarter phenomenon](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.02235/full)," a slump in mood and motivation past the midpoint of isolation, and a flattening researchers have named psychological hibernation. None of these people were weak. The environment does this to strong people on a schedule you can predict.

**Therapy does not survive the light delay**

What keeps ISS crews psychologically healthy is not the checklist. It is a support apparatus that would be recognizable to any therapist: [private psychological conferences with a psychologist or psychiatrist every two weeks](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9519135/), video calls with family, care packages on every resupply ship, an IP phone that astronauts single out as the best morale equipment on the station. Every element of it assumes a conversation can happen in real time, because Earth is 250 miles away.

On a Mars transit the one-way radio delay stretches to [as much as 22 minutes](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20220013418), with a solar-conjunction blackout severing contact entirely for about two weeks every twenty-six months. Synchronous therapy, the load-bearing wall of the current system, simply ends. So does evacuation: there is no bringing a crewmember home 160 days early from interplanetary space. Whatever psychiatry exists on a Mars ship has to work asynchronously, autonomously, or not at all.

The replacement toolkit exists mostly as prototypes. A NASA-funded team at Dartmouth spent two decades building the [Virtual Space Station](https://home.dartmouth.edu/news/2014/10/houston-we-have-problembut-no-worries-our-virtual-therapist-it), self-guided, computer-delivered programs for depression, interpersonal conflict, and stress, built around a validated six-session protocol called Problem-Solving Treatment, precisely because a Mars crew will have to be its own therapist. A [2024 review of automated psychotherapy for spaceflight](https://www.i-jmr.org/2024/1/e58803) maps how conversational AI might carry more of that load, and researchers have proposed flying [transcranial magnetic stimulation as a deep-space countermeasure](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41526-024-00401-8) for depression that medication cannot reach. Meanwhile the medications themselves face the problem this series flagged before: [most of the current formulary expires within the length of a Mars round trip](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41526-024-00414-3). Promising, all of it. Validated for the mission that needs it, none of it, by NASA's own accounting.

**A new kind of passenger, screened by no one**

Career astronauts are the best-case population: selected partly for psychological resilience, trained for years, monitored biweekly, and still, when Boeing's Starliner failed, two of them watched [an eight-day test flight become 286 days in orbit](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/crew-9-astronauts-return-earth/). Butch Wilmore and Suni Williams handled it with visible grace, as did Frank Rubio when a coolant leak stretched his six-month mission [to 371 days](https://www.livescience.com/space/space-exploration/boeing-starliner-astronauts-will-spend-at-least-240-days-in-space-is-that-a-new-record). But the lesson is not that humans are fine; it is that spaceflight schedules are promises the hardware does not always keep, and the psychological contract can be rewritten mid-mission by a helium leak.

Now remove every safeguard those astronauts had. Under the regulatory "learning period" of [51 U.S.C. § 50905](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Commercial_Space_Launch_Competitiveness_Act), commercial spaceflight participants fly by informed consent, with no required medical screening standard of any kind, and that includes psychiatric screening: no assessment before flight, no behavioral standard of care during it, no follow-up after. The previous essay in this series argued that we licensed the launch and left the medicine to a waiver. Psychiatry is the starkest case, because mental health is the one domain where the historical astronaut-selection funnel was doing nearly all of the medical work, and commercial spaceflight has removed the funnel. Private stations planned for the 2030s advertise stays of weeks or months for paying customers. Some of those customers will bring depression, anxiety, or a first panic attack at apogee, because ordinary people do, at ordinary base rates, and nothing in federal law requires anyone to have thought about it in advance. Even the best-screened flyers are not exempt after landing: Buzz Aldrin came home from the Moon into [depression and alcoholism he spent years fighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin) and later chaired a national mental-health organization, and no rule today entitles a returning spaceflight participant to so much as a follow-up phone call.

**The largest therapy desert is on Earth**

Strip space psychiatry to its problem statement and read it again slowly: deliver mental-health care to a person who cannot reach a clinician in real time, may resist reporting symptoms for fear of consequences, and must be treated with self-guided tools, asynchronous support, and a small formulary. That is not an exotic scenario. That is [the more than 120 million Americans living in federally designated mental-health professional shortage areas](https://www.kff.org/other-health/state-indicator/mental-health-care-health-professional-shortage-areas-hpsas/). It is the [65 percent of rural counties with no psychiatrist at all](https://www.ruralhealthresearch.org/recaps/20). The farmer who won't self-report to anyone in a small town where the clinic knows his family is the astronaut protecting flight status, at scale.

The transfer is not hypothetical. The problem-solving treatment built for the Virtual Space Station has [already been commercialized as a terrestrial digital therapeutic](https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2018/hm_5.html). The private psychological conference, a scheduled video session with a distant clinician, is simply telepsychiatry with better branding, and Medicare has now made its own version [permanent for behavioral health: care at home, with no geographic restriction, audio-only if needed](https://telehealth.hhs.gov/providers/telehealth-policy/telehealth-policy-updates). But Congress left a trapdoor: the statutory requirement for an in-person visit before tele-mental-health care is only [waived through December 31, 2027](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/telehealth-faq-updated-02-26-2026.pdf). When it snaps back, the patients it cuts off first are the ones the space program's whole behavioral toolkit was designed for, the people farthest from a clinician. We would be building asynchronous psychiatry for Mars while re-imposing a synchronous, in-person gate on rural Kansas.

**Write the psychiatry before the postcards**

The duct-tape checklist is not a scandal. It is what a system looks like when it plans for the last five percent of a crisis and improvises the first ninety-five. The fix is the same pattern this series keeps prescribing: build the delivery system before you sell the capability. For spaceflight, that means validated autonomous mental-health care, honest surveillance decoupled from career punishment, and a behavioral floor under commercial passengers, all cheaper to build now than to litigate after the first psychiatric emergency on a private station. And because the tools are the same ones the emptiest counties in America are waiting for, every dollar of it pays twice.

The five pages in the checklist tell a crewmate to keep talking to the patient while applying the restraints. It is, unintentionally, the right instruction for the whole enterprise: whatever else we engineer for the people we send farthest from help, the non-negotiable part is that someone is still talking to them. We should engineer that on purpose.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **FAA Office of Commercial Space Transportation and U.S. Congress:** When the § 50905 "learning period" next comes up for renewal, pair it with a behavioral-health floor for commercial human spaceflight: require operators to (a) disclose a standardized pre-flight medical screening protocol that includes a behavioral-health component, (b) carry a defined minimum behavioral-emergency capability on any mission longer than 48 hours, and (c) report in-flight behavioral events to the same confidential FAA registry proposed for physical medical events, so the standard is built from data rather than from the first tragedy on a commercial station.

2. **NASA (Human Research Program) and Congress:** Close the gap NASA's own risk register names, countermeasures "not yet developed and validated," on a deadline tied to Artemis long-duration missions. Fund validation trials of autonomous behavioral care (self-guided problem-solving treatment, conversational AI support, onboard psychiatric formulary stability, TMS) in Antarctic and analog settings, and establish a confidential behavioral-health reporting channel firewalled from flight-assignment decisions, so surveillance measures reality instead of career incentives.

3. **CMS and Congress:** Repeal, not re-extend, the Medicare requirement for an in-person visit before tele-mental-health services, currently waived only through December 31, 2027. The permanence already granted to at-home and audio-only behavioral telehealth acknowledges that distance care works; the in-person gate contradicts it and lands hardest on the 120-plus million Americans in mental-health shortage areas.

4. **HHS (HRSA and SAMHSA), with NASA and NSF:** Treat validated space and polar behavioral-health tools, asynchronous therapy modules, self-administered cognitive and mood monitoring, digital problem-solving treatment, as a named pipeline for rural, tribal, agricultural, and other clinician-scarce populations, with a standing requirement that federally funded isolation-medicine research include a terrestrial deployment pathway and a reimbursement route for tools that meet evidence standards.
