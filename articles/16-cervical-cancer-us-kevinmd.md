# One Shot, One Swab, One Visit: The Cancer We Already Know How to End

*By George Xu & Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

In May 2025, the FDA approved the first [at-home self-collection device for cervical cancer screening](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/fda-approves-teal-healths-teal-wandthe-first-and-only-at-home-self-collection-device-for-cervical-cancer-screening-introducing-a-comfortable-alternative-to-in-person-screening-302451189.html). In January 2026, [HRSA updated its women's preventive services guidelines](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/federal-cervical-cancer-testing-guidelines-expanded-to-allow-self-administered-hpv-test) to include self-collected HPV testing for average-risk women aged 30 to 65. For the patient who has declined a pelvic exam every year for a decade, the answer to "what else can we do?" is no longer "nothing."

This matters more than it sounds, because American cervical cancer screening is going backward. The American Cancer Society reports that past-year cervical cancer screening in 2023 remained [14 percent below 2019 levels](https://pressroom.cancer.org/PostPandemicScreening), with no recovery between 2021 and 2023, even as breast and colorectal screening rebounded. In 2026 the United States expects roughly [13,490 new invasive cervical cancers and 4,200 deaths](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/types/cervical-cancer/about/key-statistics.html). The mortality rate is about 55 percent higher for Black women and 80 percent higher for Native American women than for white women. Most invasive cervical cancer in this country occurs in women who were screened inadequately or never screened at all. This is not a disease of diagnostic failure. It is a disease of people who never got in the door.

That is worth sitting with, because cervical cancer is the closest thing oncology has to a solved problem. We know its cause. We have a vaccine that prevents it, a test that catches it years before it turns dangerous, and a treatment a trained nurse can deliver in a single visit. No other major cancer offers that complete a toolkit. And we are still losing 4,200 American women a year to it, while the global picture is worse by orders of magnitude.

**The global math**

The [comparative modelling analysis in The Lancet](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(20)30068-4) that underpins the WHO elimination strategy projects that girls-only HPV vaccination, on its own, reaches elimination in only about [60 percent of 78 low-income and lower-middle-income countries](https://cdn.who.int/media/docs/default-source/cervical-cancer/brisson-et-al-2020-uploaded.pdf), and that where it does work, elimination arrives between 2074 and 2102. In the highest-burden countries, mostly in sub-Saharan Africa, vaccination alone never gets there. Screening is the difference between elimination and no elimination.

Add twice-lifetime screening and the same models reach elimination in all 78 countries, 11 to 31 years sooner. A girl born today in the wrong country could otherwise die of cervical cancer as an old woman, from a disease whose cause was identified before her mother was born.

The standard reply has been an economic shrug: vaccines cost money, screening needs clinics and speculums and labs. Every one of those objections was priced on numbers that have since collapsed, and American clinicians are now using the same tools that did the collapsing.

**Every input got cheaper**

The WHO's expert advisory group concluded in 2022 that a single HPV dose protects comparably to two. Countries moved fast: 37 were using one-dose schedules in 2023, and [89 as of January 2026](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/ivac/2026/celebrating-recent-advances-in-cervical-cancer-prevention), a majority of all countries that have introduced the vaccine. WHO credits the switch with reaching [at least 6 million additional girls in 2023 alone](https://www.who.int/news/item/04-10-2024-who-adds-an-hpv-vaccine-for-single-dose-use).

The durability question is the honest one, and it is load-bearing, because the Lancet models require long-term protection to reach elimination. The evidence is reassuring without yet being lifetime-long: in the Costa Rica vaccine trial, a single dose produced [HPV-16/18 antibodies still present at 16 years](https://academic.oup.com/jncimono/article/2024/67/329/7821493), and at 11 years a single dose was about 82 percent effective against high-risk types, statistically indistinguishable from the 84 percent and 80 percent seen with two and three doses. Strong enough to act on; thin enough to keep measuring.

Screening changed the same way. [WHO's 2021 guidelines](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK572321/) make HPV testing primary and permit self-collection, which removes the speculum entirely for the roughly nine in ten women who test negative. For those who test positive, screen-and-treat compresses the referral chain into one visit: a trained primary health worker examines the cervix, confirms the lesion is eligible, and treats it with a [portable thermal ablation device, usually without anesthesia](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK549176/). No colposcopy suite, no specialist referral, no second appointment at which programs historically lost their patients.

If that sequence sounds familiar, it should. It is the same insight behind the FDA approval and the HRSA guideline: the binding constraint on cervical cancer screening was never the assay. It was the requirement that a woman present herself for a pelvic exam.

**Australia is instructive, not magic**

Australia is furthest along, with national incidence of [6.3 per 100,000 in 2021](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-rebecca-white-mp/media/australia-on-track-to-eliminate-cervical-cancer-by-2035) against an elimination threshold of fewer than 4, and a government projection of reaching it by 2035. Some of its advantages do not transfer: it is a high-income country with universal coverage. But the operative decisions do, and they are unglamorous — school-based delivery, a national register that knows who is due, an early switch to primary HPV testing, and self-collection as a standard option.

The most useful thing about Australia is its remaining failure. Cervical cancer incidence among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women runs at [roughly twice the national rate](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/article/PIIS2468-2667(26)00005-8/fulltext), driven by long-standing screening inequity. A program built for the median woman does not automatically reach the women furthest from care. American clinicians should read that number and think about our own 55 and 80 percent mortality gaps, which persist for the same structural reason.

**The window is closing while it opens**

Here is the part that should bother anyone who thinks this is a story with a happy ending. At its [June 2025 pledging summit](https://www.gavi.org/news/media-room/world-leaders-recommit-immunisation-amid-global-funding-shortfall), Gavi raised just over $9 billion against an $11.9 billion target for 2026 through 2030, leaving a roughly $2.9 billion gap against a plan that includes reaching 120 million girls with HPV vaccine by 2030. The United States, historically Gavi's third-largest government donor, [has not honored the roughly $1.6 billion pledged for that period](https://www.kff.org/global-health-policy/the-u-s-government-gavi-the-vaccine-alliance/); Congress appropriated $300 million in each of fiscal 2025 and 2026, and the administration has not disbursed it.

So the cheapest moment in the history of cervical cancer prevention is arriving at precisely the moment the money contracts. A [global modelling study published in The Lancet in May 2026](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(26)00410-1/abstract) projects that incidence in lower-income countries, already about three times that of wealthier ones, could reach a twelvefold gap by century's end without stronger vaccination and screening. That is not a forecast about biology. It is a forecast about budgets.

**What this means on Monday**

For practicing clinicians, the near-term ask is small and concrete. Know that self-collection is now an approved, guideline-covered option, and offer it to the patients in your panel who are years overdue and have quietly declined every pelvic exam you have suggested. Screening a woman who has never been screened is worth more than re-screening a woman who is current, and we now have a tool built for exactly that patient.

For everyone else, the ask is to stop describing this as hard. Cervical cancer is preventable with one shot, detectable with one swab, and treatable in one visit. It is not waiting on science. It is waiting on a budget line that is currently being cut, and on a health system that keeps building for the patients who already show up.

The next century of cervical cancer cases will not be evidence that the disease was difficult to stop. They will be evidence that we declined to.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **Clinicians and health systems:** Operationalize self-collection now that it is FDA-approved and covered under the January 2026 HRSA guidelines. Identify never-screened and long-overdue patients in your panels and offer self-collection directly, rather than waiting for them to appear for a well-woman visit they have already skipped for years.

2. **CMS and commercial payers:** Reimburse self-collected HPV testing, including mail-out and telehealth-supported models, at parity with clinician-collected sampling. A screening modality designed for people who do not come in cannot be restricted to people who come in.

3. **The United States Congress and administration:** Disburse the $300 million appropriated for Gavi in each of fiscal 2025 and 2026 and restore the 2026–2030 pledge. This is a rare global health commitment with a published, modelled return: elimination decades earlier in the countries where vaccination alone will otherwise never achieve it.

4. **Gavi:** Convert cervical cancer elimination from campaign-cycle funding into a protected ten-year budget line covering single-dose vaccine, self-collection kits, and portable thermal ablation devices, and publish what the $2.9 billion shortfall costs in girls unvaccinated and years added to elimination.

5. **CDC, HRSA, and state health departments:** Report cervical cancer screening coverage and outcomes disaggregated by race, income, and rurality, and grade programs on the share of screen-positive women treated within 30 days. Australia's remaining twofold gap for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women, and our own 55 and 80 percent mortality gaps, are what national averages conceal.
