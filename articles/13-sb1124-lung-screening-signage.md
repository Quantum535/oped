# Put Lung Cancer Screening Information Where Tobacco Is Sold

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

You still smoke. Your doctor has not brought up lung cancer screening. You walk into a corner store for cigarettes, see the usual warnings about pregnancy and secondhand smoke, and leave without ever learning that a covered scan could find cancer while it is still treatable.

The solution is straightforward. [Senate Bill 1124](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB1124), authored by Senator Bob Archuleta with coauthors in both houses, would require the California Department of Public Health to develop standardized lung cancer screening signage and require tobacco retailers to display it at every sales location beginning January 1, 2028. The Senate [passed it 29 to 4 on May 27](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billHistoryClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB1124); the Assembly Health Committee approved it 13 to 1 on June 24, and it now sits in Assembly Appropriations with the Legislature's August 31 adjournment approaching. The result, if it passes, is predictable: more high-risk Californians would encounter eligibility information, evidence on screening effectiveness, and a direct link to cessation help in the same place they already interact with tobacco products.

**The problem the bill answers**

California already knows who bears the greatest lung cancer risk: people with a current or former smoking history in the age range covered by national screening guidelines. Yet [only about 4.5 percent of eligible Californians get screened](https://www.fightcancer.org/releases/california%E2%80%99s-lung-cancer-screening-rates-are-lowest-nation-cancer-advocates-push-change), among the lowest rates in the nation, and [only 25.9 percent of the state's lung cancers are caught at an early stage](https://www.lung.org/research/state-of-lung-cancer/states/california). Often the reason is simply that eligible people never hear a clear, actionable message. Clinician visits are short. Outreach campaigns miss the patients who do not regularly see a primary care doctor. Meanwhile, tobacco retail locations remain fixed points in community life, visited repeatedly by the very population screening programs need to reach.

**What the bill actually says**

SB 1124 amends Section 22974.5 of the Business and Professions Code and adds Sections 22974.6 and 22980.7. It does not replace clinical care. It adds a low-cost public health touchpoint that existing programs have overlooked: signage no larger than 8.5 by 4 inches, developed by the Department of Public Health on or before July 1, 2027, and available to retailers at no cost from the department's website. The sign must carry four elements: eligibility criteria for lung cancer screening, information on screening effectiveness, the toll-free number of the Kick It California cessation program, and a QR code linking to Kick It California's webpage. Beginning January 1, 2028, every retailer engaged in the sale of cigarettes or tobacco products must conspicuously display it at each retail location.

Enforcement is practical rather than theatrical. A retailer who fails to display the sign faces a $500 civil penalty per offense at a retail location, with penalties deposited into the existing Cigarette and Tobacco Products Compliance Fund. The bill avoids duplicative criminal sanctions for the same violation and keeps administration within the tobacco retail licensing system the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration already oversees.

Pairing screening information with Kick It California is especially smart policy. Cessation reduces future risk. Screening catches present disease. A single sign that routes someone to both services respects how people actually change behavior: one moment of attention, two concrete next steps.

**Addressing reasonable concerns**

Some people question whether point-of-sale signage stigmatizes people who smoke. The bill's design answers this: the required content is factual and access-oriented, in the same health-focused register California already applies to tobacco control, centered on eligibility and effectiveness rather than blame. The message is that screening exists, that it works, and that help is available by phone and QR code.

Another concern is that small retailers will struggle with compliance costs. The department will provide the signs at no charge and the statute caps their size. Display requirements mirror existing license posting rules retailers already follow. The burden is visibility, not design.

A third counterargument, and the likely reason four senators voted no, is limited evidence that signage alone raises screening rates. However, no single intervention completes the pathway. SB 1124 complements coverage reforms such as SB 1309, the Stop Cancer Early Act moving through the Assembly on a parallel track, by solving a different problem: awareness at the point of risk. A person who learns about screening at retail is more likely to ask a clinician. A person who scans the QR code may connect to cessation before the next pack.

**The path forward**

The Assembly should pass SB 1124 before the August 31 adjournment and fund the Department of Public Health to produce, distribute, and evaluate the signage program on schedule. Retail associations should communicate the January 1, 2028 compliance date now. Public health partners should track whether Kick It California call volume and screening referrals rise in ZIP codes with strong retail compliance.

The central question for California lawmakers is whether we are willing to keep spending on broad advertising while ignoring the one counter every high-risk shopper already visits. The infrastructure exists, the cost is minimal, and the audience is precisely the one screening programs struggle to reach. SB 1124 meets Californians where they are and points them toward care.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **Assembly Appropriations Committee and California State Legislature:** Pass SB 1124 (Archuleta) before the August 31 adjournment and appropriate implementation funding so the Department of Public Health can publish finalized lung cancer screening signage on its website by the statutory July 1, 2027 deadline, with eligibility language aligned to current USPSTF low-dose CT criteria for adults ages 50 to 80 with qualifying smoking history.

2. **California Department of Public Health:** Offer the signage at no cost to retailers as the statute requires, include English and Spanish versions, and publish a retailer compliance checklist by October 2027, three months before the January 1, 2028 display deadline in Business and Professions Code Section 22974.6.

3. **California Department of Tax and Fee Administration:** Train tobacco retail inspectors to verify signage placement during routine license inspections beginning January 1, 2028, and report quarterly compliance rates to the Legislature.

4. **Kick It California:** Expand call-center scripts by January 2028 to assess screening eligibility, offer referral to participating screening sites, and report de-identified referral counts annually so the state can measure SB 1124 alongside screening uptake data from the California Cancer Registry.
