# PUT LUNG CANCER SCREENING INFORMATION WHERE SMOKERS ACTUALLY GO

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

You still smoke. Your doctor has not brought up lung cancer screening. You walk into a corner store for cigarettes, see the usual warnings about pregnancy and secondhand smoke, and leave without ever learning that a covered scan could find cancer while it is still treatable.

The solution is straightforward. Senate Bill 1124 would require the California Department of Public Health to develop standardized lung cancer screening signage and require tobacco retailers to display it at every sales location beginning January 1, 2028. The result is predictable: more high-risk Californians would encounter eligibility information, evidence on screening effectiveness, and a direct link to cessation help in the same place they already interact with tobacco products.

Problem magnitude

California already knows who bears the greatest lung cancer risk: current and former smokers in the age range covered by national screening guidelines. Yet screening uptake across the country remains low, often because eligible people never hear a clear, actionable message. Clinician visits are short. Outreach campaigns miss the patients who do not regularly see a primary care doctor. Meanwhile, tobacco retail locations remain fixed points in community life, visited repeatedly by the very population screening programs need to reach.

SB 1124 does not replace clinical care. It adds a low-cost public health touchpoint that existing programs have overlooked. The bill requires signage no larger than 8.5 by 4 inches, available at no cost from the department's website, with four required elements: eligibility criteria for lung cancer screening, information on screening effectiveness, the toll-free number for Kick It California, and a QR code linking to the department's cessation page.

What SB 1124 does well

The bill sets a clear production deadline: signage developed by July 1, 2027. Retailers engaged in the sale of cigarettes or tobacco products would conspicuously display the sign at each location starting January 1, 2028. That timeline gives the Department of Public Health room to align content with current United States Preventive Services Task Force criteria while giving retailers lead time to comply.

Enforcement is practical rather than theatrical. A retailer who fails to display the sign faces a $500 civil penalty per offense at a retail location, with penalties deposited into the Cigarette and Tobacco Products Compliance Fund. The bill avoids duplicative criminal sanctions for the same violation and keeps administration with the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration, which already oversees tobacco retail licensing.

Pairing screening information with Kick It California is especially smart policy. Cessation reduces future risk. Screening catches present disease. A single sign that routes someone to both services respects how people actually change behavior: one moment of attention, two concrete next steps.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether point-of-sale signage stigmatizes smokers. The solution is simple: SB 1124 uses the same factual, health-oriented tone California already applies to tobacco control, focused on eligibility and access rather than blame. The message is that screening exists, that it works, and that help is available by phone and QR code.

Another concern is that small retailers will struggle with compliance costs. Research shows the department will provide signs at no charge and prescribe a modest size limit. Display requirements mirror existing license posting rules retailers already follow. The burden is visibility, not design.

A third counterargument points to limited evidence that signage alone raises screening rates. However, no single intervention completes the pathway. SB 1124 complements coverage reforms such as SB 1309 by solving a different problem: awareness at the point of risk. A patient who learns about screening at retail is more likely to ask a clinician. A patient who scans the QR code may connect to cessation before the next pack.

The path forward

The Legislature should pass SB 1124 and fund the Department of Public Health to produce, distribute, and evaluate the signage program on schedule. Retail associations should communicate the January 1, 2028 compliance date now. Public health partners should track whether Kick It California call volume and screening referrals rise in ZIP codes with strong retail compliance.

The central question for California lawmakers is whether we are willing to keep spending on broad advertising while ignoring the one counter every high-risk shopper already visits. The case is clear and compelling: The infrastructure exists, the cost is minimal, and the audience is precisely the one screening programs struggle to reach. SB 1124 meets Californians where they are and points them toward care.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **California State Legislature:** Pass SB 1124 (Archuleta) and appropriate implementation funding so the Department of Public Health can publish finalized lung cancer screening signage on its website by July 1, 2027, with eligibility language aligned to current USPSTF low-dose CT criteria for adults ages 50 to 80 with qualifying smoking history.

2. **California Department of Public Health:** Order signage at no cost to retailers, include English and Spanish versions, and publish a retailer compliance checklist by October 2027, three months before the January 1, 2028 display deadline in Business and Professions Code Section 22974.6.

3. **California Department of Tax and Fee Administration:** Train tobacco retail inspectors to verify signage placement during routine license inspections beginning January 1, 2028, and report quarterly compliance rates to the Legislature.

4. **Kick It California:** Expand call-center scripts by January 2028 to assess screening eligibility, offer referral to participating screening sites, and report de-identified referral counts annually so the state can measure SB 1124 alongside screening uptake data from the California Cancer Registry.
