# THE ONLY CANCER SCREENING THAT REQUIRES A CONFESSION

**By Soneesh Kothagundla**

Here's an uncomfortable pattern in American cancer screening: the test that works gets skipped, and the tests people already believe in get used four times as often. Lung cancer screening reduces mortality by roughly the same margin as mammography and colonoscopy. The difference isn't effectiveness. It's that lung screening is the only major cancer test in America gated behind a confession.

A study published in JAMA in 2025, drawing on national CDC survey data, put a number on the gap. Among adults eligible for both lung cancer screening and either breast or colorectal screening, uptake of the breast or colorectal test ran at 65%. Uptake of the lung test, in the same people, ran at 17 to 18%. These are not two populations with different attitudes toward prevention. They are the same patients, in the same waiting rooms, accepting one scan and never being offered the other. The researchers describe them as "accessible to outreach and receptive to receiving preventive health care services" — in plain terms, people who show up. What they don't get is the one test whose eligibility begins with a doctor asking about, and a patient disclosing, a stigmatized behavior.

That single design choice does enormous quiet work. A mammogram is triggered by a birthday. A colonoscopy is triggered by a birthday. A lung scan is triggered by a conversation carrying decades of moral weight — one that physicians report skipping because it's awkward, because they assume the chart covers it, or because the visit runs out of clock. And the gatekeeping is codified: Medicare still conditions payment on a documented counseling and shared-decision-making visit, a prerequisite no other covered cancer screening carries. We built a stigma checkpoint into the reimbursement rules and then wondered why nobody gets through it.

The fix is not more awareness campaigns. It is taking the confession off the critical path — running lung screening the way America runs every screening it actually completes: by default.

Start with the record, not the conversation. Smoking history should be captured once, as structured data, and maintained like blood pressure — not re-excavated in an awkward exchange every time eligibility comes up. When the record says a patient qualifies, the system should generate the referral, the way it flags an overdue mammogram. Eligibility should be computed, not remembered.

Then invert the invitation. A patient's first contact with lung screening should be a letter — "you qualify for this scan; here's the number" — exactly how mammogram recall and mailed colorectal test kits work. Opt-out outreach, not opt-in disclosure. Health systems that have piloted this for other screenings consistently find that defaults, not persuasion, move uptake.

Then finish dismantling the checkpoint. Medicare has already loosened who may conduct the required pre-screening visit; it should remove the mandate entirely and let the scan be ordered like any other covered screening test. Shared decision-making can remain available without remaining a tollbooth.

And fix the message. Every piece of patient-facing material should carry the same sentence: if you smoked, you already qualify — there is nothing to justify. Nobody asks a 50-year-old woman to account for her body before ordering her mammogram. The intake form should extend smokers the same courtesy.

Two out of three lung cancers are still caught at a stage where five-year survival is measured in single digits — for a disease with a proven, reimbursed early-detection test. The JAMA data shows the missing patients are already in the building, already saying yes to screening. The confession requirement is the obstacle, and unlike most obstacles in medicine, this one can be deleted with policy: a coverage rule, an EHR field, and a mailed letter. The technology to save these patients has existed for a decade. What's left to remove is the referendum on their past.
