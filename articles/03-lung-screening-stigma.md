# Every Cancer Screening Starts With a Birthday. Lung Screening Starts With Shame.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

A mammogram is triggered by a birthday. A colonoscopy is triggered by a birthday. The scan that catches America's deadliest cancer is triggered by something else entirely: a confession.

Before a patient can be screened for lung cancer, someone has to surface a stigmatized history, quantify it in pack-years, and document it. Then, uniquely among covered cancer screenings, Medicare requires a formal counseling and shared-decision-making visit before it will pay for the scan. No birthday triggers this test. No default order generates it. The entire pathway runs through a conversation loaded with decades of moral judgment, and the results are exactly what you would expect.

**Same patients, same waiting rooms, one-quarter the uptake**

Lung cancer screening reduces mortality by roughly the same margin as mammography and colonoscopy. The difference between those tests is not effectiveness. It is design.

A study published in JAMA in 2025, drawing on national CDC survey data, put a number on the gap. Among adults eligible for both lung cancer screening and either breast or colorectal screening, uptake of the breast or colorectal test ran at 65 percent. Uptake of the lung test, in the very same people, ran at 17 to 18 percent. These are not two populations with different attitudes toward prevention. They are the same patients, in the same waiting rooms, accepting one scan and never being offered the other. The researchers describe them as "accessible to outreach and receptive to receiving preventive health care services." In plain terms: people who show up.

When people who already say yes to screening are saying yes to everything except the lung scan, the problem is not the patients. It is the checkpoint we built in front of the test.

**A stigma tollbooth, codified in the payment rules**

Physicians report skipping the smoking conversation because it is awkward, because they assume the chart already covers it, or because the visit runs out of clock. That is the informal barrier. The formal one is worse: Medicare still conditions payment on a documented counseling and shared-decision-making visit, a prerequisite no other covered cancer screening carries. We built a eligibility checkpoint into the reimbursement rules and then wondered why nobody gets through it.

The burden lands hardest on the patients with the least standing in the exam room. Smoking carries a social stigma that breast density and family history do not. Patients who already face bias in clinical settings must disclose a behavior they have been discouraged from discussing before they can access a lifesaving test. When screening depends on disclosure rather than on record data, the patients least comfortable speaking up are the least likely to qualify. Home monitoring dismantled a similar barrier in hypertension care. Lung screening should follow the same logic.

**The fixes are administrative, not medical**

Keep shared decision-making available for any patient who wants it. Just stop making it a tollbooth. No other covered cancer screening requires a separate counseling visit before the test can be ordered, and there is no evidence lung cancer patients are better served by one.

Do not wait for persuasion to close the gap, either. Health systems that have piloted opt-out outreach for other screenings consistently find that defaults, not encouragement, move uptake. A patient's first contact with lung screening should be a letter that reads: "You qualify for this scan. Here is the number."

And retire the idea that an awareness campaign will fix this. The JAMA data shows the missing patients are already in the building and already saying yes to screening. Awareness is not the obstacle. The disclosure requirement is.

Smoking history should be captured once, as structured data, and maintained like blood pressure. When the record says a patient qualifies, the system should generate the referral the way it flags an overdue mammogram. Medicare should remove the pre-screening visit mandate and let the scan be ordered like any other covered test. And every piece of patient-facing material should carry the same sentence: if you have a smoking history, you already qualify. No further explanation is required.

Two out of three lung cancers are still caught at a stage where five-year survival is measured in single digits, for a disease with a proven, reimbursed early-detection test. A patient's smoking history is a data point, not a moral record. The screening system should treat it like one.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services:** Remove the counseling and shared-decision-making visit as a condition of payment for lung cancer screening under NCD 210.14. Allow ordering and reimbursement of low-dose CT (CPT 71271) on the same terms as mammography and colorectal screening, with shared decision-making optional rather than mandatory.

2. **Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology:** Require smoking history as a structured, USCDI-documented data element by January 2027. Certify that EHR systems auto-generate a lung screening referral when structured smoking data meet USPSTF criteria, the same way systems flag overdue mammograms.

3. **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services and state Medicaid directors:** Establish a reimbursable opt-out mailed lung screening invitation, modeled on colorectal FIT outreach, with the first patient contact reading: "You qualify for this scan. Here is the number." Set a national uptake target of 50 percent among eligible patients by 2030, up from 17 to 18 percent in the 2025 JAMA CDC analysis.

4. **Department of Health and Human Services:** Require all Medicare- and Medicaid-funded patient materials to state explicitly: "If you have a smoking history, you already qualify. You do not need additional documentation of past smoking to receive this scan."
