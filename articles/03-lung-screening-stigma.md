# THE ONLY CANCER SCREENING THAT REQUIRES A CONFESSION

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Medicare and private insurers have built lung cancer screening behind a reimbursement checkpoint that no other covered cancer test carries. The same patients who accept mammography and colonoscopy at rates of 65 percent accept lung screening at 17 to 18 percent, not because the test fails, but because policy gates it behind confession.

The solution is straightforward. Lung cancer screening works as well as mammography and colonoscopy at reducing mortality. Yet most eligible patients never receive it because eligibility begins with a stigmatized disclosure rather than a birthday or a default order. The result is predictable: Two out of three lung cancers are still caught at a stage where five-year survival is measured in single digits, for a disease with a proven, reimbursed early-detection test.

Problem magnitude

Lung cancer screening reduces mortality by roughly the same margin as mammography and colonoscopy. The difference is not effectiveness. It is that lung screening is the only major cancer test in America gated behind a confession.

A study published in JAMA in 2025, drawing on national CDC survey data, put a number on the gap. Among adults eligible for both lung cancer screening and either breast or colorectal screening, uptake of the breast or colorectal test ran at 65 percent. Uptake of the lung test, in the same people, ran at 17 to 18 percent. These are not two populations with different attitudes toward prevention. They are the same patients, in the same waiting rooms, accepting one scan and never being offered the other. The researchers describe them as "accessible to outreach and receptive to receiving preventive health care services." In plain terms, people who show up.

The design flaw

A mammogram is triggered by a birthday. A colonoscopy is triggered by a birthday. A lung scan is triggered by a conversation carrying decades of moral weight, one that physicians report skipping because it is awkward, because they assume the chart covers it, or because the visit runs out of clock. Most importantly, the gatekeeping is codified: Medicare still conditions payment on a documented counseling and shared-decision-making visit, a prerequisite no other covered cancer screening carries. We built a stigma checkpoint into the reimbursement rules and then wondered why nobody gets through it.

The equity issue requires particular attention

Smoking carries social stigma that breast density and family history do not. Patients who already face bias in clinical settings must disclose a behavior they have been told to feel ashamed of before they can access a lifesaving test. When screening depends on confession rather than record data, the patients least comfortable in the exam room are the least likely to qualify. Home monitoring removed similar barriers in hypertension. Lung screening should follow the same logic.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether shared decision-making protects patients from unnecessary scans. The solution is simple: Keep shared decision-making available without making it a tollbooth. No other covered cancer screening requires a separate counseling visit before the test can be ordered.

Another concern is that patients will not participate without a doctor's encouragement. Research shows the opposite: Health systems that have piloted opt-out outreach for other screenings consistently find that defaults, not persuasion, move uptake. A patient's first contact with lung screening should be a letter: "You qualify for this scan. Here is the number."

A third counterargument points to awareness campaigns as the answer. However, only a single awareness campaign will not fix a system that punishes disclosure. The JAMA data shows the missing patients are already in the building, already saying yes to screening. The confession requirement is the obstacle.

The path forward

Smoking history should be captured once, as structured data, and maintained like blood pressure. When the record says a patient qualifies, the system should generate the referral the way it flags an overdue mammogram. Medicare should remove the pre-screening visit mandate and let the scan be ordered like any other covered screening test. Every piece of patient-facing material should carry the same sentence: patients with a smoking history already qualify. There is nothing to justify.

The central question for policymakers is whether we can afford to keep treating lung screening as a moral referendum on a patient's past. The case is clear and compelling: The science, the uptake data, and the equity considerations all support action. Every insurer and Medicare administrator should recognize default lung screening outreach as a critical investment in finding cancer before stigma blocks the door.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

We urge the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services to remove the shared-decision-making visit as a prerequisite for lung cancer screening payment and treat the scan like any other covered cancer test.

We urge Medicare and Medicaid to require smoking history as structured electronic health record data and to reimburse opt-out mailed screening invitations.

We urge the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force and state Medicaid programs to align outreach policy with mammography and colorectal screening defaults, not stigma-based disclosure.

We urge the Department of Health and Human Services to require patient-facing materials stating that patients with a smoking history already qualify and need not justify past behavior to access screening.
