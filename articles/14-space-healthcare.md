# We Can Fly Civilians to Orbit. We Have No Standard for Keeping Them Alive.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

In late 2019, a NASA astronaut two months into a six-month mission ran an ultrasound probe across her own neck for a routine fluid-shift study and found something no one had ever seen in a human in orbit: a blood clot in her left internal jugular vein. There was no protocol. There is no vascular surgeon on the International Space Station, no CT scanner, no blood bank. NASA reached a hematologist at the University of North Carolina, [Dr. Stephan Moll, the only outside physician it consulted](https://www.med.unc.edu/medicine/news/the-ultimate-telemedicine-stephan-moll-helps-treat-astronauts-blood-clot-during-nasa-mission/), and for the next three months the astronaut treated herself: performing her own ultrasounds, injecting a blood thinner drawn from the station's limited supply, guided in near real time by a radiology team 250 miles below. It worked, and it was [written up in the New England Journal of Medicine](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMc1905875).

It worked for two reasons that are about to stop being true. She was 250 miles up, close enough that images reached Earth instantly and a resupply ship could carry oral medication to her within weeks. And she was a career astronaut, screened and trained to a standard that has governed human spaceflight for sixty years. Neither condition holds for where human spaceflight is going next, and American policy has not noticed.

**The fastest way home is already half a day**

In [January 2026, for the first time in the station's twenty-five years, a crew came home early for a medical reason](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/01/15/crew-11-safely-splashes-down-after-shortened-mission/). Crew-11 undocked after a crewmember's medical episode and splashed down off San Diego roughly ten and a half hours later. That is the escape hatch working as designed, and it is worth sitting with the number: the fastest realistic evacuation from low Earth orbit still takes most of a day. From the Moon, a return takes days. From Mars, it takes months, if a launch window exists at all, because Earth and Mars line up for a trip home only about [once every twenty-six months](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20220013418). There is no ambulance to Mars.

We are building the vehicles far faster than the medicine that has to ride inside them. And in the one arena where the medical problem has already arrived, with ordinary civilians flying to space in growing numbers, the United States has chosen, deliberately, to write no rules at all.

**A new kind of patient, and no standard of care**

For most of the space age, an "astronaut" meant a genetically screened, hyper-fit test pilot, one of a tiny cohort. Roughly [700 people in all of human history have been to space](https://www.astronomy.com/space-exploration/how-many-people-have-gone-to-space/). That population is now inverting. As of [February 2026, 188 people had flown as commercial spaceflight participants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_astronaut), private citizens on SpaceX, Blue Origin, Virgin Galactic, and Axiom flights, nearly all of them since 2021. The next wave of people leaving Earth will increasingly be older, wealthier customers with the medical histories ordinary bodies carry: hypertension, coronary disease, diabetes, prior cancers.

Here is the part that should stop a policymaker cold. There is no federal medical standard for any of them. Under the "learning period" written into the Commercial Space Launch Amendments Act of 2004 and codified at [51 U.S.C. § 50905](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Commercial_Space_Launch_Competitiveness_Act), the FAA is barred from issuing regulations governing the safety of commercial crew and spaceflight participants except in narrow circumstances. It was meant as a temporary runway for a young industry. It was extended through 2023 by the 2015 SPACE Act and has been renewed since. Under it, spaceflight participants fly by informed consent: the operator must tell them, in writing, that the government has not certified the vehicle as safe, and they sign. There is no required medical screening standard, no in-flight standard of care, no floor. A commercial passenger who has a cardiac event at apogee has whatever medicine the operator chose to put on board, and no more.

This is the exact policy pattern this series keeps documenting, transplanted to orbit: a capability deployed years ahead of the system that makes it survivable. We licensed the launch and left the medicine to a waiver.

**The body keeps a ledger, and it does not fully balance**

The reassuring half of the science is real. When SpaceX's all-civilian Inspiration4 crew flew for three days in 2021 and donated their bodies to the largest space-medicine dataset ever assembled (the [Space Omics and Medical Atlas, published across the Nature journals in 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07639-y)), the headline was recovery: [more than 93 percent of the molecular disruption reversed within months](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07648-x). Ordinary people can take a short trip to orbit and their biology springs back.

The alarming half is what does not spring back, and it scales with exactly the thing a Mars mission adds: time, and radiation. About [70 percent of long-duration astronauts develop Spaceflight-Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome](https://www.nasa.gov/reference/risk-of-spaceflight-associated-neuro-ocular-syndrome-sans/) (swelling of the optic nerve and flattening of the eyeball from fluid shifting toward the head), a condition space invented and NASA now ranks among its top human risks for Mars, with globe flattening documented years after landing. Astronauts [lose 1 to 1.5 percent of weight-bearing bone density every month](https://www.nasa.gov/reference/risk-of-spaceflight-induced-bone-changes/), roughly ten times the rate of postmenopausal osteoporosis, and [some do not recover their trabecular bone even two years after return](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-13461-1). A 2024 study of astronaut kidneys found that spaceflight remodels the organ itself; its authors, modeling a Mars-duration radiation dose in mice, warned that [a crew could need dialysis on the way back](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/news/2024/jun/would-astronauts-kidneys-survive-roundtrip-mars). Radiation is the wall: NASA's career exposure limit is [about 600 millisieverts](https://www.nationalacademies.org/news/nasa-should-update-astronaut-radiation-exposure-limits-improve-communication-of-cancer-risks), and a single round-trip Mars mission is modeled at roughly 1,000: the mission breaks the standard before it launches.

And the medicine cabinet itself decays. A 2024 analysis of the space station's formulary found that [54 of 91 stocked medications have a shelf life of 36 months or less](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41526-024-00414-3) (about the length of a Mars round trip), and radiation degrades many of them faster than their Earth expiration dates predict. The pharmacy expires en route.

**Earth cannot run the clinic**

Everything that saved the astronaut with the blood clot depended on Earth being close. On a Mars transit, the one-way radio delay stretches to [as much as 22 minutes, up to 40-plus minutes round trip](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250000703), and every twenty-six months a two-week communications blackout falls as the Sun passes between the planets. Real-time telemedicine simply ends. NASA's response is a paradigm it calls [Earth-independent medical operations](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250000009) (the deliberate transfer of medical authority from the ground to the crew), and its own risk board rates the medical-care problem near the top of its scale for a Mars mission, one of the few risks marked "requires mitigation" rather than "accepted."

The scale of what would have to be handled onboard is not small. A [2026 review in the British Journal of Surgery](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13016761/) estimated that a seven-person Mars crew would face a serious medical event roughly once every 2.4 years, and catalogued 27 conditions in NASA's models that could require surgery, mostly acute abdominal emergencies and trauma. Surgery in microgravity is its own frontier: blood does not pool, it domes and forms drifting spheres, so operating means a sealed enclosure and restraints for patient, surgeon, and every instrument. Remote surgery from Earth is off the table past the Moon: teleoperation degrades badly beyond about [700 milliseconds of lag](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9536636/), and Mars is minutes. The workarounds are early: a two-pound robot called [spaceMIRA cut simulated tissue aboard the station in February 2024](https://news.unl.edu/article/surgical-robot-passes-space-test-with-flying-colors), remotely piloted from Nebraska through a sub-second delay, and a Johns Hopkins robot has [sutured bowel autonomously in pigs](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/scirobotics.abj2908). To cover the gap where no physician is aboard, NASA and Google are building an [AI clinical assistant that in 2025 testing diagnosed simulated conditions with 74 to 88 percent accuracy](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/public-sector/how-google-and-nasa-are-testing-ai-for-medical-care-in-space). Promising. Not yet a doctor.

**The destination that matters most is Earth**

Here is the turn that should reframe the whole debate, and the reason this is not a story about a billionaire's hobby. Strip space medicine down to its problem statement (deliver expert-grade care to a patient with no specialist present, no hospital, no evacuation, on miniaturized equipment, guided from far away or by no one) and you have described the daily reality of rural and low-resource medicine on Earth.

The transfer is already happening. The remotely-guided ultrasound technique proven on the station, in which a layperson is talked through a scan by an expert elsewhere, is being deployed to underserved communities; in one rural program, time to a diagnostic echocardiogram [fell from 114 days to 26](https://journal.houstonmethodist.org/articles/10.14797/mdcvj.1645). Microgravity turns out to be an ethical fast-forward button on aging: it induces osteoporosis and muscle wasting in months, reversibly, and the "[mighty mice](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2014716117)" that kept their muscle in orbit on a myostatin blocker are a live lead for treating age-related decline in the people who never leave the ground. Companies are manufacturing pharmaceuticals in orbit that are hard to make under gravity ([Varda returned a capsule of the HIV drug ritonavir in 2024](https://www.space.com/varda-space-microgravity-pharmaceutical-production-success)), and formulation research on the station has pointed pharma toward easier, lower-volume versions of blockbuster drugs. Bioprinting soft tissue is easier without gravity pulling it apart, which is why [live human heart tissue has been printed on the station](https://issnationallab.org/facilities/biofabrication-facility/) and liver constructs are being grown against the transplant shortage. A handheld analyzer that runs a lab panel from a [single drop of blood](https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2013/hm_6.html) was built because a Mars crew cannot carry a laboratory, and a clinic in a place that has no laboratory needs exactly that.

None of this is a reason to fly recklessly. It is the opposite. It is the reason to build the medicine on purpose, with a standard, rather than discover it in an inquest.

**Do not wait for the first death to write the first rule**

The learning period made sense in 2004, when the industry was two men and a prototype and heavy regulation might have strangled it in the cradle. It makes less sense with 188 civilians already flown, commercial stations slated to replace the retiring International Space Station around 2030, and paying customers whose medical histories no one is required to check. The pattern this series keeps naming (a test sold before the evidence, a screen funded ahead of the treatment) has a spaceflight form: a launch licensed ahead of the medicine. The fix is the same each time. Build the delivery system before you sell the capability, and measure the thing that actually keeps a person alive.

The astronaut with the clot in her neck got a doctor because she was close enough to Earth to reach one. The whole project of the next fifty years is to fly people to where that is no longer possible. We should decide what care they are owed before we get there, and recognize that the answer, engineered well, is also owed to the millions on Earth who already live beyond reach of a hospital.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **FAA Office of Commercial Space Transportation and U.S. Congress:** Do not extend the § 50905 "learning period" a further cycle without pairing it with a baseline occupant-health framework. Before the next renewal, require commercial human-spaceflight operators to (a) disclose a standardized pre-flight medical screening protocol, (b) publish the medical capability carried on each flight, and (c) report every in-flight medical event to a confidential FAA registry, modeled on aviation's near-miss reporting, so a standard is built from data rather than after a fatality.

2. **NASA (Human Research Program / Exploration Medical Capability) and Congress:** Fund Earth-independent medical operations (autonomous diagnostics, radiation-stable pharmaceuticals, robotic and AI-assisted care) as explicit dual-use infrastructure, with a standing requirement that every funded capability be evaluated for terrestrial rural and disaster-medicine transfer. Set a target that half of major space-medicine awards carry a documented Earth-deployment pathway by 2030.

3. **U.S. Food and Drug Administration and NASA:** Establish a formal pathway for space-relevant drug-stability and shelf-life data, given that most of the current in-flight formulary expires within a Mars round trip. Prioritize radiation-hardened formulations of the essential medications a crew cannot resupply.

4. **HHS (HRSA) and CMS:** Treat validated space-medicine tools (remotely-guided ultrasound, single-drop blood diagnostics, autonomous clinical decision support) as a pipeline for underserved rural and tribal health systems. Reimburse and deploy the ones that meet evidence standards, so the public investment in keeping astronauts alive also reaches the Americans who live farthest from a hospital.
