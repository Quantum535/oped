# California Should Finish the Job on Lung Cancer Follow-Up

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

You finally get the low-dose CT scan your doctor ordered. The result comes back abnormal. Then the bills arrive: a diagnostic CT, a PET scan, a biopsy, each with its own copay and deductible. You delay the next appointment because rent is due. By the time the workup is complete, a curable finding has moved to a stage where treatment is far harder.

The solution is straightforward. [Senate Bill 1309, the Stop Cancer Early Act](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB1309), authored by Senator Susan Rubio, would require California-regulated health plans and insurance policies to cover follow-up lung cancer screening and diagnostic services with zero patient cost sharing. The Senate [passed it 38 to 0 on May 19](https://www.fightcancer.org/releases/california-senate-advances-bill-eliminate-cost-barriers-help-patients-detect-lung-cancer). The Assembly Health Committee approved it 15 to 0 on June 17. Not one legislator in either house has voted against it. And yet the bill's fate is genuinely uncertain, because on July 1 it was placed on the Assembly Appropriations Committee's suspense file, the quiet ledger where California bills are held for a single August hearing at which they advance or die without floor debate.

**The problem the bill answers**

Lung cancer remains the leading cause of cancer death in California, [claiming nearly 10,000 lives each year](https://sd22.senate.ca.gov/news/senator-susan-rubio-introduces-stop-cancer-early-act-combat-californias-most-fatal-cancer). When the disease is found early, treatment works far better. Yet the American Lung Association's State of Lung Cancer report finds that [only 25.9 percent of California cases are caught at an early stage, ranking the state 43rd in the nation](https://www.lung.org/research/state-of-lung-cancer/states/california), and nearly half of patients are not diagnosed until stage 4. California has one of the lowest lung cancer incidence rates in the country. It finds the cancers it does have too late.

Part of that failure is the initial scan: only about [4.5 percent of eligible Californians get screened](https://sd22.senate.ca.gov/news/senator-susan-rubio-introduces-stop-cancer-early-act-combat-californias-most-fatal-cancer), compared with 70 to 75 percent uptake for other screenable cancers. But the gap this bill targets is everything that must happen after an abnormal or indeterminate result. Research links longer intervals between an abnormal finding and treatment with upstaging and worse survival, and cost is one reason those intervals stretch. A patient who cannot afford the next test does not disappear from the statistics. They appear later, at a higher stage.

**What the bill actually says**

SB 1309 adds Section 1367.58 to the Health and Safety Code and Section 10123.78 to the Insurance Code. For plan contracts and policies issued, amended, or renewed on or after January 1, 2027, it requires coverage for follow-up services provided after an initial abnormal or indeterminate result, and it names them: diagnostic computed tomography, PET/CT, tissue sampling, biopsy, bronchoscopy, pathology, and surgical consultation. Those are the steps that turn a screening alarm into a treatment plan. The bill prohibits copayments, coinsurance, deductibles, and any other form of cost sharing on them, and it ties coverage to services recommended by a health care provider acting within the scope of practice. For high-deductible plans, cost sharing survives only to the extent federal rules require it to preserve the plan's qualified status.

The design mirrors existing California policy that already requires coverage for generally accepted cancer screening tests. SB 1309 closes the loophole that lets insurers pay for the first test while leaving the confirmatory workup to patient wallets, the same design flaw that has plagued colorectal and breast pathways nationally: coverage for the screen, silence on the answer.

**The arithmetic the Appropriations Committee will weigh**

The suspense file exists to catch bills with large price tags. This is not one of them. The independent [California Health Benefits Review Program analysis](https://www.chbrp.org/sites/default/files/bill-documents/SB1309/Bill%20Analysis%20Report-%20California%20Senate%20Bill%201309%20Lung%20Cancer.pdf) estimated that the mandate would raise commercial premiums by less than 31 cents per member per month, while avoiding more than $149,000 in first-year treatment costs for each patient shifted to an earlier stage. Early detection is not only better medicine. In a state facing persistent cost pressure, it is smarter spending.

**Addressing reasonable concerns**

Some people question whether removing cost sharing will increase unnecessary follow-up testing. The bill already answers this: coverage applies only to services a provider recommends, within scope of practice, after an initial abnormal or indeterminate result. It does not open the door to elective imaging. It removes a financial barrier on a pathway clinicians already ordered.

Another concern is that premium increases, however small, add up across a large market. The alternative is far costlier. Treating advanced lung cancer consumes hospital days, systemic therapy, and lost productivity on a scale no thirty-cent line item can match. The bill asks insurers to fund the cheaper moment in the disease course.

A third counterargument points to federal and Medicare rules outside state reach. However, California regulates a large share of private coverage through the Department of Managed Health Care and the Department of Insurance. State action here sets a practical standard for millions of enrollees and gives employers a clear compliance target as contracts renew.

**August is the whole ballgame**

A bill that no legislator has opposed can still fail in California, silently, at a suspense hearing. The Assembly Appropriations Committee will decide SB 1309's fate in August, weeks before the Legislature adjourns on August 31. If it clears suspense and the Assembly floor, the Governor has until September 30 to sign it. Every abnormal lung screen should trigger the same expectation patients already have after a positive mammogram or stool test: the next medically necessary step will be covered. The science, the fiscal analysis, and two unanimous votes all point the same way. The Stop Cancer Early Act is the rare bill that spends pennies to save lives, and it has one committee left to convince.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **Assembly Appropriations Committee:** Move SB 1309 (Rubio) off the suspense file at the August determination hearing and to the floor in its current form, rejecting amendments that would allow deductibles, copayments, or coinsurance on follow-up lung cancer diagnostic services after an abnormal or indeterminate screening result.

2. **California State Assembly:** Pass SB 1309 before the August 31 adjournment so the Governor can sign it by the September 30 deadline and the mandate can take effect for contracts issued, amended, or renewed on or after January 1, 2027.

3. **Department of Managed Health Care and California Department of Insurance:** Issue joint guidance within 90 days of enactment clarifying that Section 1367.58 (Health and Safety Code) and Section 10123.78 (Insurance Code) require zero cost sharing on the listed follow-up services, so plans update policy language before the effective date rather than during the last renewal cycle.

4. **Governor's office:** Sign SB 1309 upon passage and direct DHCS, DMHC, and CDI to report annually on early-stage lung cancer diagnosis rates and follow-up completion within 90 days of an abnormal low-dose CT, with a target of raising California's early-stage diagnosis rate above the national average by 2030.
