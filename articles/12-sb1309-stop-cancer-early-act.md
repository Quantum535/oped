# CALIFORNIA SHOULD FINISH THE JOB ON LUNG CANCER FOLLOW-UP

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

You finally get the low-dose CT scan your doctor ordered. The result comes back abnormal. Then the bill arrives: a diagnostic CT, a PET scan, a biopsy, each with its own copay and deductible. You delay the next appointment because rent is due. By the time the workup is complete, a curable finding has moved to a stage where treatment is far harder.

The solution is straightforward. Senate Bill 1309, the Stop Cancer Early Act, would require California-regulated health plans and insurance policies to cover follow-up lung cancer screening and diagnostic services with zero patient cost sharing. The California Senate has already passed it unanimously. The result is predictable: fewer Californians will drop out of the screening pathway at the step where early detection actually becomes a diagnosis.

Problem magnitude

Lung cancer remains the leading cause of cancer death in California, claiming nearly 10,000 lives each year. When the disease is found early, treatment works far better. Yet fewer than 26 percent of lung cancer cases in the state are diagnosed at an early stage, and more than 70 percent of cases are still caught only after symptoms appear, when cure is uncommon.

The gap is not only the initial scan. It is everything that must happen after an abnormal or indeterminate result. Under SB 1309, follow-up services recommended by a clinician would include diagnostic CT, PET/CT, tissue sampling, biopsy, bronchoscopy, pathology review, and surgical consultation. Those are the steps that turn a screening alarm into a treatment plan. Today, cost sharing at each step pushes patients off the path.

Research demonstrates that delays matter clinically. Studies link longer intervals between abnormal findings and treatment with upstaging and worse survival. Cost is one reason those intervals stretch. A patient who cannot afford the next test does not disappear from the statistics. They appear later, at a higher stage.

What SB 1309 does well

SB 1309 applies to health care service plan contracts and health insurance policies issued, amended, or renewed on or after January 1, 2027. It prohibits copayments, coinsurance, deductibles, and any other form of cost sharing for required follow-up services. For high-deductible plans, it aligns with federal rules so the protection can take effect without breaking qualified plan requirements.

The bill mirrors existing California policy that already requires coverage for generally medically accepted cancer screening tests. SB 1309 closes the loophole that lets insurers pay for the first test while leaving the confirmatory workup to patient wallets. That is the same design flaw that has plagued colorectal and breast pathways nationally: coverage for the screen, silence on the answer.

An independent analysis prepared for the California Health Benefits Review Program estimated that the mandate would increase commercial premiums by less than 31 cents per member per month while avoiding more than $149,000 in first-year treatment costs for each patient shifted to an earlier stage. Early detection is not only better medicine. In a state facing persistent cost pressure, it is smarter spending.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether removing cost sharing will increase unnecessary follow-up testing. The solution is simple: SB 1309 ties coverage to services recommended by a health care provider acting within the scope of practice, after an initial abnormal or indeterminate result. It does not open the door to elective imaging. It removes a financial barrier on a pathway clinicians already ordered.

Another concern is that premium increases, however small, add up across a large market. Research shows the alternative is far costlier. Treating advanced lung cancer consumes hospital days, systemic therapy, and lost productivity on a scale no thirty-cent line item can match. The bill asks insurers to fund the cheaper moment in the disease course.

A third counterargument points to federal and Medicare rules outside state reach. However, California regulates a large share of private coverage through the Department of Managed Health Care and the Department of Insurance. State action here sets a practical standard for millions of enrollees and gives employers a clear compliance target as contracts renew.

The path forward

The Assembly should pass SB 1309 and send it to the governor without weakening its cost-sharing prohibition. Health plans should begin updating policy language now for the January 1, 2027 effective date rather than waiting for the last renewal cycle. Every abnormal lung screen should trigger the same expectation patients already have after a positive mammogram or stool test: the next medically necessary step will be covered.

The central question for California lawmakers is whether we can afford to keep paying for the opening scan while billing patients for the chapter that determines survival. The case is clear and compelling: The science, the fiscal analysis, and the unanimous Senate vote all support action. The Stop Cancer Early Act is the rare bill that spends pennies to save lives.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **California State Assembly:** Pass SB 1309 (Rubio) in its current form and reject amendments that would allow deductibles, copayments, or coinsurance on follow-up lung cancer diagnostic services after an abnormal or indeterminate screening result.

2. **Department of Managed Health Care and California Department of Insurance:** Issue joint guidance by September 2026 clarifying that Section 1367.58 (Health and Safety Code) and Section 10123.78 (Insurance Code) require zero cost sharing on listed follow-up services for contracts and policies issued, amended, or renewed on or after January 1, 2027.

3. **Covered California and large plan sponsors:** Require actuarial certification that SB 1309 implementation stays within the CHBRP-estimated premium impact of less than $0.31 per member per month in the commercial market.

4. **Governor's office:** Sign SB 1309 upon passage and direct DHCS, DMHC, and CDI to report annually on early-stage lung cancer diagnosis rates and follow-up completion within 90 days of an abnormal low-dose CT, with a target of raising California's early-stage diagnosis rate above 26 percent by 2030.
