# STOP COUNTING CANCER SCREENINGS. START COUNTING THE DAYS TO TREATMENT.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Nearly half of all cancer deaths worldwide in 2022 did not have to happen. That is the central estimate of a study published in The Lancet Global Health in February 2026, led by researchers at the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Of roughly 9.3 million cancer deaths that year, 4.5 million, or 48 percent, were avoidable with tools that already exist. About 3.1 million traced to five preventable risk factors, led by tobacco. A further 1.4 million were treatable: deaths that earlier detection or existing treatment, already standard somewhere in the world, could have prevented.

Those treatable deaths, breast and cervical cancer above all, cluster overwhelmingly in countries with low and medium Human Development Index scores. The instinctive response of the global health establishment is to send more screening. That instinct is the mistake, and the proof is written into the programs' own scorecards.

**A perfect score for finding untreatable cancer**

Over two decades, the tools of early detection, including portable ultrasound, HPV tests, and AI-assisted readers, have become dramatically cheaper and more mobile. The treatment systems behind them have not scaled at anything like the same pace. So the standard failure mode of global cancer aid now looks like this: a screening program reaches a rural clinic, finds a cancer that clinic cannot treat, and moves on.

That program has not saved a life. It has converted a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one, and added months of knowing. Yet by current reporting standards, it scores perfectly. Donors count screenings delivered, coverage achieved, devices deployed. A program that finds a hundred untreatable cancers posts flawless numbers while the treatable-death count stands still. The metric cannot tell the difference between medicine and paperwork.

Why does aid keep funding detection ahead of treatment? Partly because of how the money moves: time-limited pilots on three-year grant cycles, while a pathology workforce or a functioning referral network is a fifteen-year build. But mostly because of what gets counted. Activity metrics rise smoothly. Outcome metrics tell you things you do not want to hear.

**The number that cannot be gamed**

There is a single metric that would reorganize the entire field: the interval from a positive test to the first day of treatment, measured in days, for every program, published annually.

That number is unfakeable. It exposes the missing pathologist, the expired referral, and the six-hundred-kilometer journey to the nearest oncologist. It cannot be inflated by mailing more kits or parking a screening van in one more district. It measures the only thing a woman with a positive test actually cares about: how long she waits between the worst news of her life and the moment someone starts treating her.

The companion rule is just as simple: no screening scale-up should be funded until a working, financed treatment endpoint is verified inside the referral network. Call it the treatment-anchored expansion rule. It is the oncology equivalent of refusing to build an on-ramp to a bridge that is not there.

The predictable objections have predictable answers. Can one metric capture a complex health system? It does not have to; it has to capture the failure that kills people, and days-to-treatment does exactly that. Does treatment infrastructure take too long to build? All the more reason to finance it on decade horizons now: pathologists, surgical oncologists, and radiotherapy physicists take ten to fifteen years to train and retain, and no three-year grant has ever produced one. Is treatment too expensive? Most of the treatable-death toolkit consists of off-patent essential medicines, the exact category global health already buys at scale through pooled procurement of vaccines and antiretrovirals.

**Change the number, change the system**

Donors should replace "screenings delivered" with days-to-treatment as the headline metric, adopt the treatment-anchored expansion rule for every detection grant, finance the oncology workforce on decade horizons, and extend pooled procurement to the off-patent cancer drug list.

None of this is technically hard. It is only uncomfortable, because the current metrics flatter everyone involved. But 1.4 million people a year are dying of cancers the world already knows how to treat, and the programs that found their tumors got full credit for it. Donors like numbers that go up. Patients need one number to go down: the days between a positive test and the first day of treatment. Fund that number, and the rest of the system will rearrange itself around saving lives.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **Global Fund, Gates Foundation, USAID, and World Bank:** Replace "screenings delivered" as the lead outcome indicator with median days from positive screen to first treatment. No program receiving more than $10 million should report success unless that interval is published annually. Target: under 60 days in funded districts.

2. **World Bank and bilateral agencies:** Adopt a treatment-anchored expansion rule for every cancer detection loan: no screening scale-up until an independent audit verifies surgery, chemotherapy, or radiotherapy capacity within 90 days and 600 kilometers of the screening site.

3. **World Health Organization and national health ministries:** Finance pathology, surgical oncology, and radiotherapy physics training on fifteen-year horizons. Bar oncology detection grants shorter than ten years unless they include a bonded workforce retention plan.

4. **Gavi, the Global Fund, and WHO:** Extend pooled procurement to the off-patent essential cancer medicines that account for most of the 1.4 million treatable deaths identified in the February 2026 Lancet Global Health analysis. Publish price per course by country within 24 months.
