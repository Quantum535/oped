# GLOBAL CANCER AID IS MEASURING THE WRONG NUMBER

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Here's a finding that should be reordering global health priorities and
mostly isn't: nearly half of all cancer deaths worldwide in 2022 didn't
have to happen. That is the central estimate of a study published in The
Lancet Global Health in February 2026, led by researchers at the
International Agency for Research on Cancer: of roughly 9.3 million
cancer deaths that year, 4.5 million, 48%, were avoidable with tools
that already exist. About 3.1 million traced to five preventable risk
factors, led by tobacco. A further 1.4 million were treatable: deaths
that earlier detection or existing treatment, already standard somewhere
in the world, could have prevented. Those treatable deaths, breast and
cervical cancer above all, cluster overwhelmingly in countries with low
and medium Human Development Index scores.

The instinctive response is to send more screening. And that is where
global cancer policy keeps making a measurable mistake, because the
tools of early detection, portable ultrasound, HPV tests, AI-assisted
readers, have gotten dramatically cheaper and more mobile over two
decades, while the treatment systems behind them have not scaled at
anything like the same pace. A screening program that reaches a rural
clinic and finds a cancer that clinic cannot treat has not saved a life.
It has converted a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one, and
added months of knowing.

Why does aid keep funding detection ahead of treatment? Partly because
of how the money moves, time-limited pilot programs on three-year grant
cycles, while a pathology workforce or a functioning referral network is
a fifteen-year build. But mostly because of what gets counted. Global
programs report screenings delivered, coverage achieved, devices
deployed, activity metrics that rise smoothly while the treatable-death
count stands still. By those numbers, a program that finds a hundred
untreatable cancers scores perfectly. The metric cannot tell the
difference between medicine and paperwork.

---

## What to Do

So the single highest-leverage fix is almost embarrassingly simple:
change the number. The headline metric for every global early-detection
program should be the interval from positive test to treatment
initiation, how long, in days, between the moment a woman learns
something is wrong and the moment someone starts treating it. That
number is unfakeable. It exposes the missing pathologist, the expired
referral, the six-hundred-kilometer journey to the nearest oncologist,
all the failures that coverage statistics are structurally blind to.
Health systems manage what they are measured on; right now they are
measured on tests.


Three further steps follow from the first. Adopt a treatment-anchored
expansion rule: no screening scale-up is funded until a working,
financed treatment endpoint is verified inside the referral network, 
the oncology equivalent of not building an on-ramp to a bridge that
isn't there. Finance the workforce on decade horizons: pathologists,
surgical oncologists, radiotherapy physicists take ten to fifteen years
to train and retain, and no three-year grant has ever produced one. And
pool procurement of the off-patent essential cancer medicines that make
up most of the treatable-death toolkit, the way global health already
pools vaccines and antiretrovirals, so treatment capacity stops being
priced as a luxury import.

---

None of this needs a discovery. The Lancet Global Health researchers are
explicit that the 1.4 million treatable deaths they counted are
treatable with tools already deployed successfully elsewhere. The two
numbers worth holding together are 48% (the share of the world's cancer
deaths that were avoidable) and however many days currently sit between
a positive test and a first treatment in the places those deaths
concentrate. The first number tells us the scale of the failure. The
second, if we ever start publishing it, tells us exactly where the
failure lives. Early detection only becomes medicine when that
second number is small enough for the patient to still be curable when
it ends.

---

## Take Action

Every global cancer program should publish one number: days from
positive screen to first treatment. Funders should withhold scale-up
money until a financed treatment endpoint exists in the referral
network. Measure the bridge, not the billboard.
