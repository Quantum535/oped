# GLOBAL CANCER AID IS MEASURING THE WRONG NUMBER

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Global cancer donors report screenings delivered while treatable deaths remain unchanged. A woman in a rural clinic can test positive and still never reach treatment because the program that found her cancer was never required to fund the bridge to care.

The solution is straightforward. Global cancer programs should measure the time from a positive test to the first day of treatment. Yet most donors still report screenings delivered, coverage achieved, and devices deployed. The result is predictable: Programs that find untreatable cancers score perfectly while the treatable-death count stands still.

Problem magnitude

Nearly half of all cancer deaths worldwide in 2022 did not have to happen. That is the central estimate of a study published in The Lancet Global Health in February 2026, led by researchers at the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Of roughly 9.3 million cancer deaths that year, 4.5 million, or 48 percent, were avoidable with tools that already exist. About 3.1 million traced to five preventable risk factors, led by tobacco. A further 1.4 million were treatable: deaths that earlier detection or existing treatment, already standard somewhere in the world, could have prevented.

Most importantly, those treatable deaths, breast and cervical cancer above all, cluster overwhelmingly in countries with low and medium Human Development Index scores.

The measurement failure

The instinctive response is to send more screening. That is where global cancer policy keeps making a measurable mistake. The tools of early detection, portable ultrasound, HPV tests, and AI-assisted readers, have gotten dramatically cheaper and more mobile over two decades, while the treatment systems behind them have not scaled at anything like the same pace.

A screening program that reaches a rural clinic and finds a cancer that clinic cannot treat has not saved a life. It has converted a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one, and added months of knowing. By current reporting standards, a program that finds a hundred untreatable cancers scores perfectly. The metric cannot tell the difference between medicine and paperwork.

Why aid keeps funding detection ahead of treatment

Partly because of how the money moves: time-limited pilot programs on three-year grant cycles, while a pathology workforce or a functioning referral network is a fifteen-year build. But mostly because of what gets counted. Global programs report activity metrics that rise smoothly while the treatable-death count stands still.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether a single metric can capture complex health systems. The solution is simple: The headline metric for every global early-detection program should be the interval from positive test to treatment initiation, measured in days between the moment a woman learns something is wrong and the moment someone starts treating her. That number is unfakeable. It exposes the missing pathologist, the expired referral, and the six-hundred-kilometer journey to the nearest oncologist.

Another concern is that treatment infrastructure takes too long to build. Research shows the opposite of rushing screening first: No screening scale-up should be funded until a working, financed treatment endpoint is verified inside the referral network. That is the oncology equivalent of not building an on-ramp to a bridge that is not there.

A third counterargument points to the cost of training oncologists and pathologists. However, only decade-long financing produces a workforce that lasts. Pathologists, surgical oncologists, and radiotherapy physicists take ten to fifteen years to train and retain, and no three-year grant has ever produced one.

The path forward

Donors should change the number they report, adopt a treatment-anchored expansion rule, finance the workforce on decade horizons, and pool procurement of the off-patent essential cancer medicines that make up most of the treatable-death toolkit, the way global health already pools vaccines and antiretrovirals.

The central question for policymakers is whether we can afford to keep funding billboards while the bridge remains unfinished. The case is clear and compelling: The science, the economics, and the accountability logic all support action. Every global health funder should recognize test-to-treatment time as a critical investment in making early detection actually save lives.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

We urge every global cancer donor to replace screenings-delivered metrics with days from positive test to treatment initiation.

We urge the World Bank, bilateral agencies, and philanthropic funders to withhold screening scale-up until a financed treatment endpoint is verified in the referral network.

We urge WHO and national health ministries to finance oncology workforce development on decade horizons, not election-cycle grants.

We urge pooled procurement bodies to negotiate off-patent essential cancer medicines the way global health already pools vaccines and antiretrovirals.
