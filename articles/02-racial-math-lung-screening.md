# Colorblind Cancer Screening Isn't Fairness. It's How Black Patients Get Missed.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

Two numbers should end a comfortable assumption in American medicine. Under the lung cancer screening criteria most hospitals use today, the odds that a Black patient's future cancer would have been flagged in advance were, in a large national cohort, as low as 46 percent. For white patients in the same cohort, several of the same criteria caught 72 to 80 percent. Identical rules, applied identically to everyone, delivering results that are nowhere near identical.

This is not a story about patient behavior, and it is not a story that better outreach can fix. It is a story about what happens when medicine confuses treating people the same with serving them equally, and about a policy establishment that has never required screening rules to prove they work for the people they will be applied to.

**What the cohort showed**

The numbers come from a study published in JNCI: Journal of the National Cancer Institute, led by Adoma Manful and Melinda Aldrich. It followed 52,667 adults with a smoking history, 65 percent of them Black, through the Southern Community Cohort Study and tested ten screening strategies against fifteen years of actual diagnoses: 1,689 lung cancers. This was not a database of who happened to get screened. It was a direct examination of the eligibility formulas themselves, checked against who actually got sick.

Most strategies flagged fewer future Black patients than future white patients. And the worst performer was the newest. The American Cancer Society's 2023 update, presented as a refinement, produced the largest racial sensitivity gap of any strategy tested: 18 percentage points.

**Deleting the word does not delete the bias**

Here is the finding that should reorder the national conversation. Removing race from the models entirely, the race-blind approach that sounds like fairness, made the disparity worse, not better. A model built primarily on data from one population inherits that population's patterns and applies them uniformly where they do not hold. Striking the word "race" from the formula does not strike the bias from the training data. It only hides where the bias came from.

What closed the gap was the opposite approach. Race-aware calibration, which deliberately adjusts eligibility thresholds to cancel out the disparity the uniform formula produces, lifted sensitivity to 71 to 73 percent for both Black and white patients and cut the gap between Black and white women from 23 percentage points to 5. Same test, same technology, same budget. The only thing that changed was whether the math was required to work for everyone.

**The reflex that became a blind spot**

American medicine is midway through a necessary movement to strip race out of clinical algorithms, and often rightly, because race frequently operated as a crude penalty on Black patients. But the movement has hardened into an automatic response: any race term in any formula is now suspect. The lung screening data exposes the flaw in that reflex. There is a difference between deleting race while pretending the bias left with it, and using race deliberately to cancel a measured bias out. In this case, race-blind policy is not neutrality. It is a policy choice that reallocates missed cancers onto Black patients, in quantities a peer-reviewed study has now counted.

Calibration should be a bridge, not a destination. Adopt calibrated thresholds now, because they are published, validated against real outcomes, and the only tested approach that closes the gap. Then fund the diverse research cohorts that will let race eventually be retired from the formula, replaced by the true predictors it currently stands in for.

**No more algorithms without subgroup receipts**

Health systems do not need to wait for a guideline cycle to act. Every large system already holds the electronic records to audit whom its screening criteria flag, by race, this quarter. The ones that look will find what the study found. And guideline bodies should adopt one universal rule: no eligibility algorithm, anywhere in medicine, without published performance broken out by subgroup. The Southern Community Cohort that produced this study is the template for the diverse data the country needs, and it should not be nearly as rare as it is.

Every year of unchanged criteria is another year of systematically missed Black patients. The rules in place today were never validated on the people they fail. That is no longer an unknown. It is a documented decision, renewed every guideline cycle we let it stand.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force and American Cancer Society:** Adopt the race-calibrated lung screening thresholds from the 2026 JNCI Southern Community Cohort analysis (Manful, Aldrich, et al.) in the next guideline cycle. Target at least 71 percent sensitivity for Black and white patients and no more than a 5 percentage-point gap between Black and white women (down from 23 points under uniform rules).

2. **Every federal guideline body:** Bar adoption of any screening algorithm unless the sponsoring organization publishes sensitivity and specificity for Black patients, white patients, and women separately. The American Cancer Society's 2023 update produced an 18 percentage-point racial gap. No update should take effect without subgroup tables.

3. **National Cancer Institute:** Fund at least five new cohort studies modeled on the Southern Community Cohort Study (52,667 adults, 65 percent Black) within the next appropriations cycle, with minority enrollment no lower than the population the criteria will serve.

4. **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services:** Add a Hospital Compare metric requiring every hospital receiving Medicare dollars to report, quarterly, the percentage of eligible Black patients and eligible white patients its lung screening criteria flag. Publish the racial gap alongside the number.
