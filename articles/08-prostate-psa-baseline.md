# THE ANSWER TO THE PSA MESS IS ONE BLOOD TEST AT 45

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force restricted PSA screening in 2012 using harm models from an era before active surveillance and MRI-first pathways. Metastatic prostate cancer then rose more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74, and guideline policy has not caught up to modern treatment.

The solution is straightforward. A single baseline PSA in a man's mid-forties predicts his lifetime risk of lethal prostate cancer decades in advance. Yet the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force still frames the question as whether to screen at all, not how to screen smart. The result is predictable: Metastatic prostate cancer rose more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 between 2010 and 2018 after testing fell nationwide.

Problem magnitude

In 2012, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommended against routine PSA screening for prostate cancer for all men. Testing fell nationwide. A 2022 JAMA Network Open analysis found metastatic prostate cancer rose more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 between 2010 and 2018. Five-year survival for metastatic disease is around one in three, versus nearly 100 percent when caught localized. The American Cancer Society's statistics show the same curve from another angle: incidence climbing again since 2014, driven entirely by advanced-stage disease.

The public argument about this has calcified into two camps shouting past each other: bring back screening, versus remember the harms. Both are living in the wrong decade. The way out is not choosing a side. It is changing the question from whether to screen to how.

What the evidence already shows

The skeptics' history is real. The 2012 recommendation answered a genuine catastrophe: annual PSA testing was routing huge numbers of men with slow-growing cancers into surgery and radiation, with incontinence and impotence as the price of treating a disease that would never have killed them. But the harm side of that ledger has since collapsed. In 2010, most men diagnosed with low-risk prostate cancer were treated immediately. Today roughly 60 percent choose active surveillance, up from under 20 percent, and MRI-first biopsy pathways filter out trivial cancers before they are ever diagnosed.

Research demonstrates that long-running Swedish cohort studies have shown a single baseline PSA drawn in a man's mid-forties predicts his risk of lethal prostate cancer decades in advance. That one number sorts men into futures: the majority, with low baselines, need retesting only every five years or more and may safely stop early; the small high-baseline minority, who go on to account for roughly half of eventual prostate cancer deaths, get watched closely. This is not the 1990s model of testing every man every year, and it is not the 2010s model of testing no one. It is one tube of blood at 45, risk-stratified intervals afterward, MRI before any biopsy, and surveillance instead of surgery for low-risk findings.

The equity issue requires particular attention

Black men face roughly 70 percent higher incidence and about double the mortality of white men, and were barely represented in the trials the 2012 decision rested on. The major American trial's enrollment was overwhelmingly white. A blanket "don't screen," priced on mostly white cohorts, was applied uniformly to the men with the most to lose. The Prostate Cancer Foundation broke ranks in 2023, recommending Black men begin PSA testing as early as 40.

Addressing reasonable concerns

Some people question whether one baseline test is enough. The solution is simple: Screen once, then screen smart. Low-risk men extend intervals. High-risk men receive closer follow-up. The number tells the system how hard to keep looking.

Another concern is that shared decision-making protects patients. Research shows the opposite of the 2018 grade C recommendation: outsourcing a population-level question to a fifteen-minute visit left millions of men with no guidance at all.

A third counterargument points to the harms of biopsy and treatment. However, only the 1990s model produced those harms at scale. Active surveillance and MRI-first pathways have rewritten the harm ledger since the 2012 decision.

The path forward

The USPSTF should re-review with harm models built on surveillance-era treatment patterns, adopt baseline risk-stratified screening as the framework the trials and cohorts already support, and set a tripwire so a decade of rising metastatic incidence triggers re-review automatically. A baseline-at-45 national framework, with baseline-at-40 for Black men and men with family history, would build equity in from the start.

The central question for policymakers is whether we can afford another decade of metastatic disease while two camps relitigate a settled harm story. The case is clear and compelling: The science, the surveillance revolution, and the equity considerations all support action. Every guideline body should recognize baseline PSA screening as a critical investment in finding lethal prostate cancer before it spreads.

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Re-review prostate cancer screening within 18 months using harm models that assume 60 percent active surveillance uptake and MRI-first biopsy pathways, not 2009-era immediate treatment rates. Issue a new recommendation for one baseline PSA (CPT 84153) at age 45 for average-risk men.

2. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Set baseline PSA at age 40 for Black men and for men with a first-degree family history of prostate cancer, matching the 2023 Prostate Cancer Foundation guidance. Stratify follow-up intervals by baseline value: PSA below 1.0 ng/mL, retest every five to ten years; above 3.0 ng/mL, annual or biennial monitoring per Swedish cohort protocols.

3. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Establish an automatic re-review trigger if SEER data show metastatic prostate cancer incidence rising more than 20 percent over any five-year period following a screening restriction, as it did more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 after the 2012 recommendation.

4. **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services:** Add baseline PSA and risk-stratified PSA follow-up to the Medicare preventive services benefit with zero cost-sharing. Stop treating screening as an unfunded "discuss with your doctor" item in a fifteen-minute visit.
