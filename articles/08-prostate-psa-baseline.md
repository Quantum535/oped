# We Stopped Testing for Prostate Cancer. Metastatic Cases Jumped 40 Percent.

*By Soneesh Kothagundla*

---

In 2012, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommended against routine PSA screening for prostate cancer for all men. Testing fell nationwide, exactly as intended. Then came the bill. A 2022 JAMA Network Open analysis found metastatic prostate cancer rose more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 between 2010 and 2018. Five-year survival for metastatic disease is around one in three, versus nearly 100 percent when the cancer is caught localized. The American Cancer Society's statistics show the same curve from another angle: incidence climbing again since 2014, driven entirely by advanced-stage disease.

The public argument about this has calcified into two camps locked in opposition. Bring back screening, says one. Remember the harms, says the other. Both are living in the wrong decade. The way out is not choosing a side. It is changing the question, from whether to screen to how.

**The harm ledger the 2012 decision was built on no longer exists**

The skeptics' history is real, and it should be said plainly: the 2012 recommendation answered a genuine catastrophe. Annual PSA testing was routing huge numbers of men with slow-growing cancers into surgery and radiation, with incontinence and impotence as the price of treating a disease that would never have caused them serious harm.

But the harm side of that ledger has since collapsed. In 2010, most men diagnosed with low-risk prostate cancer were treated immediately. Today roughly 60 percent choose active surveillance, up from under 20 percent, and MRI-first biopsy pathways filter out trivial cancers before they are ever diagnosed. The overtreatment machine the Task Force feared has been largely dismantled. Guideline policy is still priced against a world that no longer exists.

**One number, drawn once, that sorts men into futures**

Long-running Swedish cohort studies have shown that a single baseline PSA drawn in a man's mid-forties predicts his risk of lethal prostate cancer decades in advance. That one number sorts men into futures. The majority, with low baselines, need retesting only every five years or more and can safely stop early. The small high-baseline minority, who go on to account for roughly half of eventual prostate cancer deaths, get watched closely.

This is not the 1990s model of testing every man every year, and it is not the 2010s model of testing no one. It is one tube of blood at 45, risk-stratified intervals afterward, MRI before any biopsy, and surveillance instead of surgery for low-risk findings. Each piece is already validated. Only the framework connecting them is missing.

**The men with the most to lose were left out of the evidence**

Black men face roughly 70 percent higher incidence and about double the mortality of white men, and they were barely represented in the trials the 2012 decision rested on; the major American trial's enrollment was overwhelmingly white. A blanket "don't screen," priced on mostly white cohorts, was applied uniformly to the men with the most to lose. The Prostate Cancer Foundation broke ranks in 2024, recommending Black men begin PSA testing as early as 40. A national baseline framework should build that in from the start: baseline at 45 for average-risk men, baseline at 40 for Black men and men with a family history.

Nor is the current compromise defensible. The Task Force's 2018 grade C recommendation outsourced a population-level question to a fifteen-minute clinic visit under the banner of shared decision-making, which in practice left millions of men with no guidance at all. And the old fear of biopsy harms describes the 1990s pipeline, not an MRI-first one.

**Change the question, then set a tripwire**

The Task Force should re-review prostate cancer screening using harm models built on surveillance-era treatment patterns, adopt baseline risk-stratified screening as the framework the trials and cohorts already support, and set an automatic tripwire so that a sustained rise in metastatic incidence triggers re-review instead of waiting on the political weather.

A decade of rising metastatic disease is what the last screening war cost. One blood test at 45, read intelligently, is what ending it costs. The evidence has already left both camps behind. Policy should catch up before another cohort of men learns their diagnosis was late by design.

---

## A Plea to Policymakers

1. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Re-review prostate cancer screening within 18 months using harm models that assume 60 percent active surveillance uptake and MRI-first biopsy pathways, not 2009-era immediate treatment rates. Issue a new recommendation for one baseline PSA (CPT 84153) at age 45 for average-risk men.

2. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Set baseline PSA at age 40 for Black men and for men with a first-degree family history of prostate cancer, matching the 2024 Prostate Cancer Foundation guidance. Stratify follow-up intervals by baseline value: PSA below 1.0 ng/mL, retest every five to ten years; above 3.0 ng/mL, annual or biennial monitoring per Swedish cohort protocols.

3. **U.S. Preventive Services Task Force:** Establish an automatic re-review trigger if SEER data show metastatic prostate cancer incidence rising more than 20 percent over any five-year period following a screening restriction, as it did more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 after the 2012 recommendation.

4. **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services:** Add baseline PSA and risk-stratified PSA follow-up to the Medicare preventive services benefit with zero cost-sharing. Stop treating screening as an unfunded "discuss with your doctor" item in a fifteen-minute visit.
