# THE ANSWER TO THE PSA MESS IS ONE BLOOD TEST AT 45

**By Soneesh Kothagundla**

Here's a natural experiment no ethics board would have approved: in 2012, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommended against routine PSA screening for prostate cancer — for all men. Testing fell nationwide. A decade later the results are in the registries: a 2022 JAMA Network Open analysis found metastatic prostate cancer — five-year survival around one in three, versus nearly 100 percent when caught localized — rose more than 40 percent among men 45 to 74 between 2010 and 2018. The American Cancer Society's statistics show the same curve from another angle: incidence climbing again since 2014, driven entirely by advanced-stage disease, with the share of men diagnosed after the cancer has spread roughly doubling from its low.

The public argument about this has calcified into two camps shouting past each other: bring back screening, versus remember the harms. Both are living in the wrong decade — and the way out is not choosing a side. It is changing the question from whether to screen to how.

The skeptics' history is real. The 2012 recommendation answered a genuine catastrophe: annual PSA testing was routing huge numbers of men with slow-growing cancers into surgery and radiation — incontinence and impotence as the price of treating a disease that would never have killed them. But the harm side of that ledger has since collapsed. In 2010, most men diagnosed with low-risk prostate cancer were treated immediately; today roughly 60 percent choose active surveillance, up from under 20 percent, and MRI-first biopsy pathways filter out trivial cancers before they're ever diagnosed. The task force's 2018 half-reversal — a grade C, "discuss it with your doctor" — priced in none of this. It simply outsourced a population-level question to whatever remains of a fifteen-minute visit.

Here is what neither camp says: the evidence for a third option has existed for years. Long-running Swedish cohort studies have shown that a single baseline PSA drawn in a man's mid-forties predicts his risk of lethal prostate cancer decades in advance. Not this year's cancer — his lifetime trajectory. That one number sorts men into futures: the majority, with low baselines, need retesting only every five years or more and may safely stop early; the small high-baseline minority — who go on to account for roughly half of eventual prostate cancer deaths — get watched closely. This is not the 1990s model of testing every man every year, and it is not the 2010s model of testing no one. It is one tube of blood at 45, risk-stratified intervals afterward, MRI before any biopsy, and surveillance instead of surgery for low-risk findings. Screen once, then screen smart.

The equity case makes the redesign urgent rather than optional. Black men face roughly 70 percent higher incidence and about double the mortality of white men, and were barely represented in the trials the 2012 decision rested on — the major American trial's enrollment was overwhelmingly white. A blanket "don't screen," priced on mostly white cohorts, was applied uniformly to the men with the most to lose. The Prostate Cancer Foundation broke ranks in 2023, recommending Black men begin PSA testing as early as 40. A baseline-at-45 national framework — baseline-at-40 for Black men and men with family history — would build that correction in rather than bolting it on.

What has to happen is specific. The USPSTF should re-review with harm models built on surveillance-era treatment patterns, not 2009's, and adopt baseline risk-stratified screening as the framework the trials and cohorts already support. And guideline processes need a tripwire: a decade of rising metastatic incidence should trigger re-review automatically, not wait for the calendar. The deeper failure here is asymmetry — the bar for restricting a test proved far lower than the bar for restoring one, and men with treatable-turned-terminal cancers have spent ten years paying the difference. The skeptics were right that we once found too many cancers that didn't matter. The fix was never to stop looking. It was to look once, early, and let the number tell us how hard to keep looking.
