THE 50-CANCER BLOOD TEST HASN'T EARNED OUR FAITH YET
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's the most seductive proposition in modern medicine: a single $949 blood draw that screens for more than 50 types of cancer at once — including pancreatic and ovarian, the killers with no screening test at all. It's called Galleri, it has been on sale in the United States since 2021, by prescription, and it comes with two details the marketing does not lead with: it is not FDA-approved, and no study anywhere has shown that people who take it live longer than people who don't.

What the published evidence does show comes largely from PATHFINDER, a study of 6,662 adults over 50 published in The Lancet in 2023. The test flagged a possible cancer signal in about one percent of participants, and among those flagged, 38 percent turned out to have cancer. Read that number in reverse: roughly six in ten positive results were false alarms, each one launching a cascade of imaging, specialist referrals, sometimes invasive procedures, and months of dread. The test did find real cancers, including some no screening program would have caught. What the study could not show — was not designed to show — is whether finding them changed a single outcome.

That distinction is not pedantry. It is the graveyard of screening history. A test can find cancers earlier and save no one, because starting the survival clock earlier is not the same as moving the date of death — the statistical mirage called lead-time bias — and because some detected cancers would never have harmed anyone, a problem called overdiagnosis that turns healthy people into patients. The cautionary example is ovarian cancer: the UKCTOCS trial screened more than 200,000 British women and followed them for the better part of two decades. Screening shifted diagnoses meaningfully earlier. It produced no significant reduction in deaths. Earlier detection is the mechanism; mortality is the test. Ovarian screening passed the first and failed the second, and no guideline body on earth recommends it today.

The right way to find out whether Galleri is different is already underway: the NHS-Galleri trial, a randomized study of 140,000 adults in England — the largest trial of its kind ever run. And the interim signal is instructive. In 2024, after reviewing early results, NHS England declined to expand the test's rollout, choosing to wait for the trial's final data. That is what evidence-based restraint looks like: enormous enthusiasm for the question, and a refusal to skip ahead to the answer.

The United States is running the opposite play. The test reached the market ahead of FDA review under the lab-developed-test pathway, and Congress has entertained the heavily lobbied Multi-Cancer Early Detection Screening Coverage Act, which would pre-build a Medicare coverage pathway for these tests — implying billions in annual spending — before the mortality evidence exists. Coverage is being arranged first, with the plan that evidence will be along shortly. Screening history suggests that when the money arrives before the answer, the answer stops mattering.

To be clear about the standard being applied: this is not a case against screening. The strongest argument in cancer policy today is that proven screening — lung, colorectal, cervical — with randomized trial evidence of mortality benefit sits scandalously underused. But that standard cuts both ways, and Galleri has not yet met it. There is also a specific, predictable danger in a test marketed as catching everything: false reassurance. A negative Galleri result is not a license to skip a colonoscopy — the manufacturer itself says so — but a patient holding a clean 50-cancer blood test will inevitably treat the tests that catch one cancer as redundant. A test that hasn't been shown to save lives can still cost them, by displacing the ones that do.

The question was never whether the technology can find cancer; it can. The question is whether the people who take the test live longer than the people who don't, and a 140,000-person randomized trial is about to answer it. The only thing lost by waiting is a head start on a hypothesis — and if forty years of screening research teach anything, it is that hypotheses with price tags attached are exactly the ones that need answers first.
