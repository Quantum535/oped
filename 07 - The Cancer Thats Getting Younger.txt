THE QUIET SURGE OF COLORECTAL CANCER IN YOUNG ADULTS
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's a ranking that should unsettle anyone under 50: colorectal cancer is now the leading cause of cancer death among American men younger than 50, and the second-leading cause among women in that age group, behind only breast cancer. In the late 1990s it ranked fourth for both. That reshuffling, documented in the American Cancer Society's annual statistics in CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians, did not happen because other cancers became less deadly. It happened because colorectal cancer kept rising in young adults — one to two percent a year, for three consecutive decades — while the system built to catch it early kept assuming it was an older person's disease.

The generational arithmetic is stark. An analysis published in the Journal of the National Cancer Institute found that Americans born around 1990 carry roughly twice the risk of colon cancer and four times the risk of rectal cancer that Americans born around 1950 carried at the same age. The American Cancer Society's own reporting shows the share of new colorectal cancer diagnoses in people under 55 nearly doubled between 1995 and 2019, from 11 percent to 20 percent. This is a birth-cohort effect: each generation is carrying more risk than the one before it, which means something changed in the world these generations grew up in.

Nobody yet knows what. Diet, obesity, antibiotics, the gut microbiome, some unidentified environmental exposure — all are under active investigation, none is proven. The honest scientific answer is that we don't know why this is happening. But screening policy does not get to wait for etiology. We screened for cervical cancer for half a century before anyone identified HPV as the cause. When the registry data says a cancer is arriving earlier, the detection system is supposed to move to meet it.

To its credit, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force did move, lowering the recommended starting age from 50 to 45 in 2021. But eligibility is not screening. Federal survey data show only about a third of adults aged 45 to 49 are up to date on colorectal cancer screening, against roughly 70 percent of adults 50 to 75. A guideline change without a delivery system behind it is a press release. The newly eligible are not receiving mailed stool tests by default, are not being flagged at routine visits, and in many cases have no idea the age changed at all.

The sharper failure sits below 45, where patients are unscreenable by design and symptomatic ones wait. Studies of early-onset patients repeatedly document months of delay between first symptom — rectal bleeding, iron-deficiency anemia, a persistent change in bowel habits — and diagnosis, because those symptoms in a 34-year-old are attributed to hemorrhoids, stress, or diet until they can't be. The predictable result is that young patients are diagnosed disproportionately at stage III or IV, with cancers that a colonoscopy triggered by the first red-flag symptom would have caught earlier, and that in some cases began as polyps that screening could have removed before they became cancer at all.

Then there is the least excusable gap of all: by some published estimates, as many as one in four early-onset colorectal cancer patients had a family history that already qualified them for screening before 45 under existing guidelines. Those are not guideline failures. They are failures to take a five-minute family history and act on it — the cheapest risk-stratification tool in medicine, sitting unused in the intake form.

None of the fixes requires a discovery. Make 45 a real starting line, with mailed stool tests and default orders triggered by a birthday, the way 50 once was. Treat rectal bleeding in a 32-year-old as a finding that ends in a scope, not a reassurance. Collect family history systematically and let it move the screening age the way the guidelines already say it should.

The disease has moved; thirty years of registry data say so. The open question is how many more annual statistics reports it takes before the system that is supposed to catch it moves too.
