WHAT HAPPENED WHEN AMERICA STOPPED LOOKING FOR PROSTATE CANCER
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's a natural experiment no ethics board would ever have approved: in 2012, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommended against routine PSA screening for prostate cancer — not for any subgroup, for all men. Testing fell nationwide. A decade later, the results of that experiment are sitting in the registry data, and they are not subtle. Metastatic prostate cancer — the kind with a five-year survival around one in three, versus nearly 100 percent for cancer caught while still localized — has been rising ever since.

The numbers are consistent across independent analyses. A 2022 study in JAMA Network Open found the incidence of metastatic prostate cancer among men aged 45 to 74 rose by more than 40 percent between 2010 and 2018. The American Cancer Society's annual statistics tell the same story from a different angle: after two decades of decline, overall prostate cancer incidence began climbing again around 2014 — roughly three percent a year — with the increase driven by advanced-stage diagnoses, and the share of men diagnosed with distant-stage disease roughly doubling from its early-2010s low. The cancers didn't stop coming when the tests did. They just stopped being found in time.

An honest accounting has to start by conceding that the 2012 recommendation was not reckless. PSA-driven overdiagnosis was real: the test was routing large numbers of men with slow-growing cancers that would never have harmed them into surgery and radiation, with incontinence and impotence as the price of treating a disease they were never going to die from. The task force weighed documented harms against a mortality benefit that looked thin in flawed trials, and made a defensible call on the data it had.

But the harm side of that ledger has since collapsed. In 2010, most American men diagnosed with low-risk prostate cancer were treated immediately. Today roughly 60 percent choose active surveillance — monitoring the cancer rather than treating it — up from under 20 percent a decade earlier, and MRI-guided biopsy pathways now filter out many trivial cancers before they are ever diagnosed. The central cost of finding a prostate cancer, the reflex to overtreat it, has shrunk dramatically. The guideline's 2018 partial reversal — a grade C, meaning men 55 to 69 should decide individually with their doctor — acknowledges this in the weakest available form: a recommendation to have a conversation, delegated to whatever is left of a fifteen-minute visit.

The cost of the screening pause was also not distributed evenly. Black men face roughly 70 percent higher prostate cancer incidence and about double the mortality of white men — and they were barely represented in the trials the 2012 recommendation rested on; the major American trial's enrollment was overwhelmingly white. A decision priced on mostly white cohorts was applied uniformly to the group with the most to lose, the same structural error the lung cancer screening literature has now documented in its own eligibility formulas. In 2023 the Prostate Cancer Foundation broke with the wait-and-see posture and recommended that Black men begin PSA discussions as early as 40. The federal guideline has yet to follow.

There is a pattern here worth naming, because it recurs across American screening policy: guidelines swing hard on harm data, then correct at a glacial pace while the outcome curves bend the wrong way. A framework that moved decisively against overtreatment in 2012 has now watched metastatic incidence rise for over a decade without a comparably decisive response. The bar for restricting a test, it turns out, is much lower than the bar for restoring one.

The screening skeptics were right that the PSA era caught too many cancers that didn't matter. The registry data now shows we purchased the mirror-image problem: missing cancers that do. A guideline process that treated the first error as urgent and treats the second as an agenda item is not practicing evidence-based medicine. It is practicing loss aversion, and men with treatable-turned-terminal cancers are paying for it.
