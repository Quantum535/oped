WHY SOME CANCERS GET SCREENED MORE THAN OTHERS
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's an uncomfortable pattern in American cancer screening: the test that works gets skipped, and the tests people already believe in get used four times as often. That is not a difference in effectiveness. Lung cancer screening reduces mortality by roughly the same margin as mammography and colonoscopy. It is a difference in who we think deserves to be caught early.

A study published in JAMA in 2025, drawing on national CDC survey data, put a number on the gap. Among adults eligible for both lung cancer screening and either breast or colorectal cancer screening, uptake of the breast or colorectal test ran at 65%. Uptake of the lung cancer test, in the same people, ran at roughly 17 to 18%. These are not two different populations with two different relationships to preventive medicine. They are the same patients, sitting in the same waiting rooms, agreeing to one scan and declining the other.

The researchers' own framing of the result is worth sitting with: these patients are, in their words, "accessible to outreach and receptive to receiving preventive health care services." In plain terms, the people skipping lung cancer screening are not screening-averse. They are getting their mammograms. They are getting their colonoscopies. They show up for the appointment. What they are not getting, at a rate four times lower, is the one screening test whose eligibility criteria require a doctor to first ask about, and document, a history of smoking.

That single design choice does a lot of quiet work. A mammogram or a colonoscopy is triggered by a birthday. A lung cancer screening is triggered by a conversation in which a physician has to raise a behavior that carries decades of moral weight attached to it, and in which the patient has to disclose it. Physicians report skipping the eligibility conversation because it's awkward, because they assume it's covered elsewhere in the chart, or because they simply run out of visit time before getting to the one topic on the list that isn't a number on a form. The result is that a screening test proven to save lives is being gated behind a stigma checkpoint that no other major cancer screening has to clear.

It would be more comfortable to believe that lung cancer screening lags because it's newer, or less understood, or logistically harder to access — a low-dose CT scanner instead of a mammography suite. Those factors are real, but the JAMA data rules out the simplest version of that story: if access and unfamiliarity were the whole explanation, we would not see the same patients cheerfully completing one screening and skipping the other in the very same year. Something else is doing the work, and the honest answer is that lung cancer is still, culturally, the cancer people are quietly assumed to have brought on themselves.

That assumption has a body count. Two out of three lung cancers are still caught at a stage where five-year survival is measured in single digits, largely because screening — the one intervention that reliably catches the disease early — is treated as optional in a way mammography stopped being treated as optional forty years ago. Nobody asks a 50-year-old woman to justify her body before ordering her mammogram. We ask smokers to justify their eligibility for a scan before we'll even discuss whether they qualify.

Fixing this does not require a new drug or a new machine. It requires U.S. medicine to stop running lung cancer screening as a referendum on a patient's past choices and start running it the way it runs every other early-detection test: as a number tied to age and risk, offered by default, with the stigma stripped out of the intake form. The technology to save these patients already exists and is already reimbursed. What's missing is the willingness to offer it as automatically as we offer the tests aimed at diseases we've decided not to blame anyone for.
