WHY GLOBAL CANCER DEATHS ARE RISING FASTEST IN POORER COUNTRIES
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's a finding that should be reordering global health priorities and mostly isn't: nearly half of all cancer deaths worldwide in 2022 didn't have to happen. That is the central estimate from a population-based study published in The Lancet Global Health in February 2026, led by researchers at the International Agency for Research on Cancer, which calculated exactly how many of the roughly 9.3 million cancer deaths that year were avoidable through primary prevention, earlier detection, or treatment that already exists. The number came out to 4.5 million deaths — 48% of the total.

The study split that figure into two distinct categories, and the split matters. Roughly 3.1 million deaths, or 33% of the total, were attributable to five preventable risk factors: tobacco use, alcohol consumption, excess body weight, chronic infections, and ultraviolet exposure. A further 1.4 million deaths, or 14%, were classified as treatable — cases where earlier detection or curative treatment, using tools that already exist and are already in use somewhere in the world, could have changed the outcome. Lung cancer accounted for the largest share of preventable deaths, at 1.1 million, more than a third of all preventable deaths combined. Female breast cancer accounted for the largest share of treatable deaths, at roughly 200,000.

The detail that turns this from a global statistic into an equity finding is where the treatable deaths cluster. The researchers found disproportionately large shares of avoidable deaths from cervical and breast cancer specifically in countries with low or medium Human Development Index scores. These are not, for the most part, cancers where the science is unsettled or the technology exotic. They are cancers with decades of trial evidence behind their detection and treatment protocols, concentrated as avoidable deaths in the places least equipped to act on that evidence.

This is the asymmetry sitting underneath most global cancer initiatives: the tools for early detection — portable ultrasound, HPV testing, AI-assisted cytology readers — have gotten dramatically cheaper and more mobile over the past two decades. The health-system infrastructure required to act on a positive result once you find it has not scaled at anywhere near the same pace. A screening program that reaches a rural clinic and finds a cancer that clinic cannot treat converts a late diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one. The Lancet Global Health data suggests this is exactly what's happening at scale: detection tools reaching further than the treatment systems that are supposed to receive their results.

Part of the explanation is how international cancer aid tends to be structured. It is disproportionately organized as time-limited pilot programs, renewed or discontinued on multi-year grant cycles. Building a sustained pathology workforce, or a mammography referral network that doesn't lapse when a grant period ends, is a project measured in training pipelines and long-term maintenance contracts — a fifteen-year undertaking financed, in practice, in three-year increments.

None of this requires a scientific breakthrough to close. The Lancet Global Health researchers are explicit that the 1.4 million treatable deaths they identified are treatable with tools that already exist and are already deployed successfully elsewhere. What's missing is not a discovery. It's the unglamorous, multi-decade investment in workforce, supply chains, and referral systems that would let low- and medium-HDI countries use the tools the rest of the world already has.

The two numbers worth holding together are these: 48% of 2022's global cancer deaths were avoidable, and the avoidable share concentrated hardest in breast and cervical cancer — in the countries with the least capacity to act on an early diagnosis. Early detection only saves the lives of patients who have somewhere to go after the test comes back positive. According to the most comprehensive accounting of avoidable cancer deaths yet published, for a very large share of the world, that somewhere still doesn't reliably exist.
