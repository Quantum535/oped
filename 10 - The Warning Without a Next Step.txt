THE NEW MAMMOGRAM WARNING THAT COMES WITHOUT A NEXT STEP
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's what quietly changed for American women in September 2024: every mammography report in the country is now federally required to tell a woman whether her breast tissue is dense. Nearly half of women over 40 will get that notice. Here's what didn't change: whether anyone will pay for the additional scan the notice implies she may need.

Dense breast tissue creates two problems at once. The first is that it hides cancer. Mammography's sensitivity — its ability to catch a cancer that is actually there — runs near 87 percent in mostly fatty tissue and falls to roughly 60 percent in extremely dense tissue, because dense tissue and tumors both appear white on the image. Radiologists describe it as looking for a snowball in a snowstorm. The second problem is that density is itself a risk factor: women with extremely dense tissue carry up to about four times the breast cancer risk of women with mostly fatty tissue. The same finding that makes the test likelier to miss a cancer makes the cancer likelier to exist.

The evidence that a next step helps is not speculative. The DENSE trial, a randomized study published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2019, offered supplemental MRI to women with extremely dense tissue and normal mammograms. Interval cancers — the ones that surface between screening rounds, after a reassuring result — fell from 5.0 per 1,000 screens to 2.5. Half of the cancers that would have announced themselves as a lump between mammograms were instead caught by the scan.

So consider the sequence American policy has now assembled. The federal government mandates telling a woman her mammogram may have missed something. A randomized trial says what to do about it. And then the policy simply stops: no federal requirement that insurers cover supplemental imaging, a patchwork of state laws — some mandating coverage, most not, many leaving cost-sharing intact — and an out-of-pocket price for a breast MRI that easily exceeds a thousand dollars. The notification is federally guaranteed. The answer is means-tested.

It's worth remembering why the rule exists at all. It was not medicine's idea; it was a patient's. Nancy Cappello, a Connecticut woman, was diagnosed with stage 3 breast cancer weeks after a normal mammogram and learned only afterward that her dense tissue could have hidden the tumor — and that her doctors knew. The notification movement she started produced Connecticut's first-in-the-nation law in 2009 and reached 38 states before the FDA finally federalized it. But that movement always had two halves: tell women, and cover the follow-up. The federal government adopted the half that was free.

The Find It Early Act, pending in Congress, would supply the other half, requiring insurers to cover supplemental and diagnostic breast imaging without cost-sharing. Insurers counter that supplemental MRI has not yet shown a mortality reduction in a randomized trial — which is true, and which would be a more principled objection if the coverage it defends weren't already full of tests held to no such standard. More to the point, the federal government has already adjudicated the question that matters: it decided density information is important enough to mandate into every mammography report in America. Mandating the warning while leaving the response to a patient's disposable income is not a coherent screening policy. It is a disclosure regime wearing the costume of one.

The predictable result, absent a fix, is a two-tier system sorted by income rather than by risk: women who can absorb a four-figure bill act on the notice, and women who can't simply carry the anxiety. Telling a woman her all-clear might be wrong is half a policy. The other half is giving her a way to find out that doesn't depend on her credit limit. Right now, the warning arrives by federal mandate. The answer arrives by invoice.
