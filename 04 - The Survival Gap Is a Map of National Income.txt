WHAT BREAST CANCER SURVIVAL REVEALS ABOUT GLOBAL INEQUALITY
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's a statistic that should be a global scandal and instead barely registers: a woman diagnosed with breast cancer in North America has roughly an 84% chance of being alive in five years. A woman diagnosed with the identical cancer in parts of the low-income world faces a five-year survival estimate as low as 40%. This is not a difference in tumor biology. Breast cancer does not become more lethal when it crosses the equator. According to a modelling study published in The Lancet Global Health, it is a difference in whether anyone found the cancer while it was still small enough to cure — and in whether treatment was there to finish the job once it was found.

The Lancet Global Health study modeled early detection and treatment strategies for breast cancer specifically in low-income and upper-middle-income countries and found five-year survival in these settings clustering between 40% and 60%, against North America's roughly 84%. The best outcomes in the model didn't come from early detection alone or treatment access alone — they came from combining both. A country that improves screening without also fixing its chemotherapy supply chain, the study found, captures only part of the possible benefit. Early detection without the medicine to treat what it finds is a diagnosis, not a cure.

The scale of this disparity was confirmed independently by the largest cancer survival study ever conducted. CONCORD-3, published in The Lancet, analyzed individual records for more than 37.5 million cancer patients across 322 population-based registries in 71 countries, covering diagnoses from 2000 to 2014. Its finding on breast cancer specifically: survival is rising almost everywhere, but the gap between the best-performing and worst-performing health systems has not meaningfully closed. A woman's five-year odds still depend heavily on which country's registry her diagnosis would be recorded in.

A more recent Lancet Global Health analysis, published in February 2026, quantifies what's actually recoverable from this gap. Examining avoidable cancer deaths worldwide in 2022, the study found that female breast cancer accounts for more treatable — as opposed to preventable — deaths than any other cancer: roughly 200,000 deaths a year, or close to 15% of all deaths worldwide that better detection and treatment could have prevented. The same analysis found that avoidable deaths from breast cancer are disproportionately concentrated in countries with low or medium Human Development Index scores. The deaths this study counts as "avoidable" are not a hypothetical — they are deaths from a cancer with a well-established, decades-old evidence base for how to catch and treat it, occurring in exactly the places where that evidence base hasn't been built into working health infrastructure.

What connects all three studies is a specific and correctable claim: breast cancer is not a cancer where the science is unsettled. The trial evidence for mammographic screening and the treatment protocols that follow a positive result are among the most established in oncology. The gap in five-year survival between Boston and Johannesburg is not a gap in medical knowledge. It is a gap in mammography units, trained radiologists to read the films, and a referral pathway that gets a positive result into a surgeon's hands inside of weeks rather than months.

There is a reflex in global health commentary to treat this as a funding problem that time and donations will solve, and funding is part of it. But the Lancet Global Health modelling data is specific about where the leverage is: outcomes improve most where early detection and treatment access are built up together, not where one arrives without the other. A screening program that finds cancer a country can't yet treat converts a delayed diagnosis into an earlier, still-fatal one.

Three separate analyses in two of the field's most rigorously reviewed journals now agree on the shape of this problem, even if none of them alone can fix it. A five-year survival rate is supposed to measure how good our medicine is. Across all three studies, in breast cancer specifically, it still measures how well-resourced your country's health system was when you got sick.
