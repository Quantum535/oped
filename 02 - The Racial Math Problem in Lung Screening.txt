THE HIDDEN GAP IN LUNG CANCER SCREENING GUIDELINES
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's a number that should stop you cold: under the screening criteria most American hospitals use today, the odds that a Black patient's future lung cancer would have been flagged in advance were, in a large national cohort, as low as 46%. For white patients in the same cohort, several of the same criteria caught 72 to 80%. That gap was published this year in JNCI: Journal of the National Cancer Institute, and it did not come from a database of who happened to get screened. It came from testing the actual eligibility formulas against fifteen years of who actually got sick.

The study, led by Melinda Aldrich and colleagues, followed 52,667 adults with a smoking history — 65% of them Black or African American — through the Southern Community Cohort Study and checked ten different lung cancer screening strategies against the 1,689 people who went on to be diagnosed. It found that most existing strategies flagged fewer future Black patients than future white patients as eligible for screening in advance. The gap wasn't marginal. Sensitivity for Black participants ranged from 46% to 73% across the strategies tested, versus 72% to 80% for white participants.

The most striking result involves the newest guideline, not the oldest one. The American Cancer Society updated its screening recommendation in 2023, a revision presented as a refinement of the 2021 USPSTF criteria. The JNCI analysis found that update produced the largest racial sensitivity gap of any strategy in the study — 18 percentage points. A guideline change adopted as progress made the disparity in who gets caught early measurably worse, and that fact was only visible once researchers tested it against real diagnoses rather than taking the revision's rationale at face value.

Here is the finding that should complicate anyone's easy intuition about how to fix this: removing race from the screening models entirely — the race-blind approach that sounds, on its face, like the fair solution — made the disparity worse, not better. The researchers describe this as a textbook case of algorithmic bias, where a model built primarily on data from one population inherits that population's patterns and applies them uniformly to a population where they don't hold. Pretending the model is race-neutral doesn't remove the bias baked into its training data; it just hides where the bias is coming from.

What did work was the opposite of colorblindness: race-aware fine-tuning, where eligibility thresholds are explicitly calibrated by race to correct for the disparity the uniform formula produces. Applied to the USPSTF and PLCOm2012 risk models, this approach lifted sensitivity to 71–73% across both racial groups and — the number that matters most — narrowed the sensitivity gap between Black and white women specifically from 23 percentage points down to 5.

That is not a subtle improvement. It is the difference between a screening program that quietly filters out a large share of the Black patients who will go on to develop lung cancer, and one that catches them at close to the same rate as everyone else. The tool to do this exists in the published literature right now. It has been tested against real long-term outcomes in a cohort large enough to trust. It has not been adopted by the bodies that write the guidelines American clinics actually follow.

There is a broader pattern worth naming plainly, one this JNCI study documents with unusual precision: screening formulas built and validated primarily on data from one population tend to underperform when applied uniformly elsewhere, and the fix requires acknowledging the disparity explicitly rather than designing around it. That is an uncomfortable prescription for a field that generally prefers guidelines that don't mention race at all. The JNCI researchers' data says the uncomfortable version works, and the comfortable version doesn't.

Every year the current criteria stay in place unmodified is a year in which Black patients, according to this analysis, are systematically less likely to be flagged for the one test that turns a fatal-stage lung cancer diagnosis into a survivable one. That is not a hypothetical equity concern. It is a specific, published, peer-reviewed number, and it is currently sitting unaddressed in front of the people with the authority to change it.
