THE SLOW PROGRESS TOWARD ELIMINATING CERVICAL CANCER
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's what makes cervical cancer different from almost every other cancer on earth: we know exactly what causes it, we have a vaccine that prevents it, and we have a screening test that catches it years before it turns dangerous. By the standards of oncology, this is close to a solved problem. A pair of modelling studies published in The Lancet in 2020 asked a direct question about that claim: if the world actually deployed what it has, on the timeline it has promised, how long would elimination take? The answer, for dozens of the countries carrying the heaviest burden, was decades longer than the public health target suggests.

The two studies, run as a comparative modelling analysis across 78 low-income and lower-middle-income countries, tested how quickly cervical cancer could be driven down to the World Health Organization's elimination threshold — fewer than 4 cases per 100,000 women, the point past which epidemiologists consider the disease to have stopped functioning as a major public health threat. Under high HPV vaccination coverage alone, the models projected elimination arriving between 2059 and 2102, depending on the country and the specific threshold used. That is not a rounding error against the WHO's stated 2030 targets. It is seventy to eighty years of additional cases and deaths beyond what the public campaign implies.

The same analysis found a lever that meaningfully changes that timeline: adding twice-lifetime screening with HPV testing, on top of vaccination, accelerated elimination by 11 to 31 years depending on the setting. That is a large, achievable gain from adding one intervention most high-income countries have already normalized. It also means the countries furthest from elimination are, disproportionately, the ones that haven't yet built the screening infrastructure that would close most of the remaining gap.

A separate Lancet Global Health analysis mapped where that infrastructure actually exists, reviewing cervical cancer screening programs and coverage across 202 countries and territories. The pattern it found lines up with the modelling results: the countries furthest behind on the elimination timeline are largely the same ones without a funded national screening program operating at scale, regardless of what their official cancer control plans say on paper. A country can adopt a screening policy that satisfies every box an international assessment checks and still leave the overwhelming majority of its at-risk women with no functioning place to get screened.

The most recent Lancet Global Health accounting of avoidable cancer deaths worldwide, published in February 2026, quantifies what that gap costs. Of the roughly 4.5 million cancer deaths in 2022 the study classifies as avoidable through prevention, earlier detection, or treatment, cervical cancer deaths are disproportionately concentrated in countries with low or medium Human Development Index scores — a pattern the researchers found repeated for breast cancer as well, but not for most other major cancers. Cervical cancer, in other words, is one of a small number of cancers where being avoidable and being unequal are, statistically, close to the same finding.

What separates the countries on track from the countries decades behind isn't a scientific gap. Australia, which paired sustained HPV vaccination with organized screening, is projected to reach elimination years ahead of the WHO's own target. The Lancet's modelling suggests most of the 78 countries studied could follow a similar trajectory with the same two interventions, deployed with the same consistency. What they have mostly received instead, per the coverage data, is a policy commitment without the multi-decade, non-donor-cycle financing that turns a policy into a functioning clinic.

There's a specific discomfort in a preventable, curable, thoroughly modeled cancer still projected to take until the next century to eliminate in large parts of the world, entirely because of a financing and infrastructure gap rather than a scientific one. The Lancet's own analysis is not ambiguous about what would close it. It has simply not yet been treated as urgent enough to fund at the pace the modelling says is possible.
