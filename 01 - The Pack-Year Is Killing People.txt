THE OVERLOOKED NUMBER BEHIND LUNG CANCER SCREENING
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's what almost nobody outside a pulmonology clinic knows: the number that decides whether Medicare will pay for the scan that could save your life has never been tested against a simple question — does it actually find the people who go on to get sick? "Pack-years," the formula at the center of every lung cancer screening guideline in the country, was adopted because it was easy to write on an intake form. Whether it is the best available predictor of who develops lung cancer is a separate question, and a study published this year in JNCI: Journal of the National Cancer Institute finally asked it directly.

Under the current U.S. Preventive Services Task Force guideline, you qualify for an annual low-dose CT scan only if you are 50 to 80 years old, have smoked at least 20 pack-years, and either still smoke or quit within the last 15 years. Miss any one of those thresholds and the screening that could catch your cancer at stage I instead of stage IV simply isn't offered to you.

The JNCI study, led by researchers including Melinda Aldrich, followed 52,667 adults with a smoking history for fifteen years and checked which of ten different screening strategies would actually have flagged the 1,689 people who went on to be diagnosed with lung cancer. Some of those strategies were the fixed rules currently in use — the 2021 USPSTF criteria, the American Cancer Society's 2023 update. Others were statistical risk-prediction models that weigh smoking history alongside age, family history, and other factors rather than applying a single hard cutoff.

The fixed rules performed worse. Sensitivity — the share of eventual lung cancer patients the criteria would have flagged in advance — ranged as low as 46% for some groups under the simple threshold approaches, compared with the 71–73% achieved by better-calibrated risk models. That gap is not a rounding error. It is the difference between catching roughly one in two future patients early and catching nearly three in four.

The part of the finding that should embarrass guideline-writers specifically is what happened with the 2023 American Cancer Society update. It was introduced as an improvement on the 2021 USPSTF criteria. The JNCI analysis found it instead produced the single largest sensitivity gap of any strategy tested — an 18-percentage-point disparity in who the criteria caught. A guideline revision, published and adopted as progress, made the underlying measurement problem worse, and nobody would have known without a study designed specifically to check.

This is the pattern worth sitting with: American screening guidelines are frequently built, revised, and adopted based on plausibility and administrative convenience rather than a rigorous test of predictive performance against real outcomes. Pack-years sounds like a measurement. It behaves, in practice, like a proxy that happens to work reasonably well for some patients and poorly for others, and no one checked which was which until a research team went back and ran the numbers against fifteen years of actual diagnoses.

There is also a simpler failure hiding in the same guideline that the JNCI study didn't need statistics to expose: the hard age cutoff at 80. Lung cancer risk does not fall off a cliff on a patient's eightieth birthday, and the exclusion has nothing to do with tumor biology — it reflects assumptions about frailty and life expectancy that vary enormously by individual and have never been the basis for an age limit on any other major cancer screening test.

None of this requires a new scientific discovery to fix. The risk-prediction models that outperformed fixed criteria in the JNCI analysis already exist, are already published, and are already sitting in the literature waiting for the USPSTF to act on them. What's required is for the people who write these guidelines to treat "does this actually work better" as a question that gets answered before a threshold is adopted, not years afterward by an outside research team.

Screening guidelines are supposed to follow the data on who actually gets sick. The JNCI study is a rare case where someone checked, and found the data wasn't the priority the guideline-writers had assumed it was.
