A SCREENING PROGRAM IS ONLY AS GOOD AS ITS FOLLOW-UP
By Soneesh Kothagundla

Here's the number that exposes the difference between running screening tests and running a screening program: in many American health systems, barely half of patients who test positive on a stool-based colorectal cancer screening test go on to receive the follow-up colonoscopy within a year. A positive stool test is not a diagnosis. It is an alarm — a flag that says there may be a cancer here, look now. In roughly half of cases, the alarm rings into an empty room.

The stakes of that silence have been quantified. A 2017 study in JAMA followed more than 70,000 Kaiser Permanente patients with positive stool-test results and compared outcomes by how long the follow-up colonoscopy took. Patients whose colonoscopy came more than twelve months after the positive result had more than twice the odds of being diagnosed with colorectal cancer, and more than three times the odds of advanced-stage disease, compared with patients scoped within a month. Delay is not a neutral administrative fact. It is the mechanism by which a curable finding matures into an incurable one.

The reflex is to file this under patient noncompliance, and it is the wrong ledger. Follow-up completion rates vary enormously from one health system to the next — which is itself the evidence that the system, not the patient, is the variable. The documented barriers are mundane: colonoscopy scheduling that requires multiple phone calls, referrals that expire before an appointment opens, no transportation, no one who explains what the positive result actually means. And until recently, a bill — the "free" screening test whose positive result triggered cost-sharing on the colonoscopy that followed. Federal rules finally closed that loophole for private insurance in 2022 and for Medicare in 2023, a fix that many patients, and a surprising number of clinics, still don't know exists.

Underneath the mundane barriers sits a design flaw: the metrics reward tests, not outcomes. Health systems and insurers report screening rates — the share of eligible patients who completed a test — and a mailed stool kit counts the moment it is returned. A system collects full credit for that kit even if the positive result dies in an electronic inbox. No national quality measure holds systems accountable for follow-up completion the way Medicare holds hospitals accountable for readmissions, so the part of screening that actually determines whether anyone survives is the part nobody is graded on.

The fix is known and unglamorous: patient navigation. Randomized trials have repeatedly shown that assigning a person — not a portal notification, a person — to shepherd patients from positive result to completed colonoscopy raises completion substantially. A navigator's salary is a rounding error against a single course of treatment for stage IV colon cancer, which is the alternative purchase.

Nor is this a colorectal peculiarity. The same pattern shows up in mammography, where abnormal results without timely diagnostic resolution cluster in safety-net populations. Wherever screening is delivered as a test rather than a pathway, closing the loop defaults to whoever has the free time, the phone minutes, the transportation, and the insurance literacy to chase it — a distribution that runs precisely inverse to medical need. The mailed stool test was celebrated, rightly, as the tool that brought colorectal screening to people who would never get a screening colonoscopy. But extending the test without extending the pathway doesn't close the gap in outcomes. It relocates the gap to the step after the test, where it is harder to see and no one is measuring.

The test has never saved anyone's life; the pathway does. Until follow-up completion is measured, published, and paid for the way the test itself is, American screening programs will keep posting rising screening rates over a widening crack — and a positive result that goes nowhere isn't early detection. It is a cancer we found and then agreed to lose track of.
